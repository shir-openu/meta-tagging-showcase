1 Introduction

In this work, we study fairnessterm·key in classificationterm·dom. Nearly all classificationterm·dom tasks face the challenge of achieving utilityterm·key in classificationterm·dom for some purpose, while at the same time preventing discriminationterm·dom against protected population subgroups. A motivating example is membership in a racial minority in the context of banking. An article in The Wall Street Journal (8/4/2010) describes the practices of a credit card company and its use of a tracking network to learn detailed demographic information about each visitor to the site, such as approximate income, where she shops, the fact that she rents children’s videos, and so on. According to the article, this information is used to “decide which credit cards to show first-time visitors” to the web site, raising the concern of steering, namely the (illegal) practice of guiding members of minority groups into less advantageous credit offerings [SA10].

We provide a normative approach to fairnessterm·key in classificationterm·dom and a framework for achieving it. Our framework permits us to formulate the question as an optimization problem that can be solved by a linear program. In keeping with the motivation of fairnessterm·key in online advertising, our approach will permit the entity that needs to classify individualsterm·key, which we call the vendor, as much freedom as possible, without knowledge of or trust in this party. This allows the vendor to benefit from investment in data mining and market research in designing its classifier, while our absolute guarantee of fairnessterm·key frees the vendor from regulatory concerns.

Our approach is centered around the notion of a task-specific similarity metricterm·key describing the extent to which pairs of individualsterm·key should be regarded as similarterm·key for the classificationterm·dom task at hand.1 The similarity metricterm·key expresses ground truth. When ground truth is unavailable, the metricterm·key may reflect the “best” available approximation as agreed upon by society. Indeed, we envision that, typically, the distance metricterm·key would be externally imposed, for example, by a regulatory body, or externally proposed, by a civil rights organization.

The choice of a metricterm·key need not determine (or even suggest) a particular classificationterm·dom scheme. There can be many classifiers consistent with a single metricterm·key. Which classificationterm·dom scheme is chosen in the end is a matter of the vendor’s utilityterm·key function which we take into account. To give a concrete example, consider a metricterm·key that expresses which individualsterm·key have similarterm·key credit worthiness. One advertiser may wish to target a specific product to individualsterm·key with low credit, while another advertiser may seek individualsterm·key with good credit.

This is when unqualified members of S are chosen, in order to “justify” future discriminationterm·dom against S (building a case that there is no point in “wasting” resources on S ). 4 Fair Affirmative Action

In this section, we explore how to implement what may be called fair affirmative action. Indeed, a typical question when we discuss fairnessterm·key is, “What if we want to ensure statistical parityterm·def between two groups S and T, but members of S are less likely to be “qualified”? 6 Discussion and Future Directions

In this paper we introduced a framework for characterizing fairnessterm·key in classificationterm·dom. The key element in this framework is a requirement that similarterm·key people be treated similarly in the classificationterm·dom. We developed an optimization approach which balanced these similarity constraints with a vendor’s loss function. and analyzed when this local fairnessterm·key condition implies statistical parityterm·def, a strong notion of equal treatment. We also presented an alternative formulation enforcing statistical parityterm·def, which is especially useful to allow preferential treatment of individualsterm·key from some group. 6.3 Does Fairnessterm·key Hide Information?

We have already discussed the need for hiding (non-)membership in S in ensuring fairnessterm·key. We now ask a converse question: Does fairnessterm·key in the context of advertising hide information from the advertiser? Statistical parityterm·def has the interesting effect that it eliminates redundant encodings of S in terms of A, in the sense that after applying M, there is no f : A → {0, 1} that can be biased against S in any way. This prevents certain attacks that aim to determine membership in S .

Unfortunately, this property is not hereditary. Indeed, suppose that the advertiser wishes to target HIV-positive people. If the set of HIV-positive people is protected, then the advertiser is stymied by the statistical parityterm·def constraint. However, suppose it so happens that the advertiser’s utilityterm·key function is extremely high on people who are not only HIV-positive but who also have AIDS. Consider a mapping that satisfies statistical parityterm·def for “HIV-positive,” but also maximizes the advertiser’s utilityterm·key. We expect that the necessary error of such a mapping will be on members of “HIV\AIDS,” that is, people who are HIV-positive but who do not have AIDS. In particular, we don’t expect the mapping to satisfy statistical parityterm·def for “AIDS” – the fraction of people with AIDS seeing the advertisement may be much higher than the fraction of people with AIDS in the population as a whole. Hence, the advertiser can in fact target “AIDS”.

Alternatively, suppose people with AIDS are mapped to a region B ⊂ A, as is a |AIDS|/|HIV positive| fraction of HIV-negative individualsterm·key. Thus, being mapped to B maintains statistical parityterm·def for the set of HIV-positive individualsterm·key, meaning that the probability that a random HIV-positive individual is mapped to B is the same as the probability that a random member of the whole population is mapped to B. Assume further that mappings to A \ B also maintains parityterm·key. Now the advertiser can refuse to do

business with all people with AIDS, sacrificing just a small amount of business in the HIV-negative community.

These examples show that statistical parityterm·def is not a good method of hiding sensitive information in targeted advertising. A natural question, not yet pursued, is whether we can get better protection using the Lipschitz property with a suitable metricterm·key.

A Catalog of Evils

We briefly summarize here behaviors against which we wish to protect. We make no attempt to be formal. Let S be a protected set.

1. Blatant explicit discriminationterm·dom. This is when membership in S is explicitly tested for and a

“worse” outcome is given to members of S than to members of S c.

2. Discriminationterm·dom Based on Redundant Encoding. Here the explicit test for membership in S is replaced by a test that is, in practice, essentially equivalent. This is a successful attack against “fairnessterm·key through blindness,” in which the idea is to simply ignore protected attributes such as sex or race. However, when personalization and advertising decisions are based on months or years of on-line activity, there is a very real possibility that membership in a given demographic group is embedded holographically in the history. Simply deleting, say, the Facebook “sex” and “Interested in men/women” bits almost surely does not hide homosexuality. This point was argued by the (somewhat informal) “Gaydar” study [JM09] in which a threshold was found for predicting, based on the sexual preferences of his male friends, whether or not a given male is interested in men. Such redundant encodings of sexual preference and other attributes need not be explicitly known or recognized as such, and yet can still have a discriminatory effect.

3. Redlining. A well-known form of discriminationterm·dom based on redundant encoding. The following definition appears in an article by [Hun05], which contains the history of the term, the practice, and its consequences: “Redlining is the practice of arbitrarily denying or limiting financial services to specific neighborhoods, generally because its residents are people of color or are poor.”

4. Cutting off business with a segment of the population in which membership in the protected set is disproportionately high. A generalization of redlining, in which members of S need not be a majority of the redlined population; instead, the fraction of the redlined population belonging to S may simply exceed the fraction of S in the population as a whole.

5. Self-fulfilling prophecy. Here the vendor advertiser is willing to cut off its nose to spite its face, deliberately choosing the “wrong” members of S in order to build a bad “track record” for S . A less malicious vendor may simply select random members of S rather than qualified members, thus inadvertently building a bad track record for S .

6. Reverse tokenism. This concept arose in the context of imagining what might be a convincing refutation to the claim “The bank denied me a loan because I am a member of S .” One possible refutation might be the exhibition of an “obviously more qualified” member of S c who is also denied a loan. This might be compelling, but by sacrificing one really good candidate c ∈ S c the bank could refute all charges of discriminationterm·dom against S . That is, c is a token rejectee; hence the term “reverse tokenism” (“tokenism” usually refers to accepting a token member of S ). We remark that the general question of explaining decisions seems quite difficult, a situation only made worse by the existence of redundant encodings of attributes.
