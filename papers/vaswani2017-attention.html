Abstract

The dominant sequenceterm·key transduction models are based on complex recurrent or convolutional neural networks that include an encoderterm·key and a decoderterm·key. The best performing models also connect the encoderterm·key and decoderterm·key through an attentionterm·key mechanism. We propose a new simple network architecture, the Transformerterm·ai, based solely on attentionterm·key mechanisms, dispensing with recurrenceterm·key and convolutionsterm·key entirely. Experiments on two machine translationterm·key tasks show these models to be superior in quality while being more parallelizableterm·key and requiring significantly less time to train. Our model achieves 28.4 BLEU on the WMT 2014 English- to-German translationterm·key task, improving over the existing best results, including ensembles, by over 2 BLEU. On the WMT 2014 English-to-French translationterm·key task, our model establishes a new single-model state-of-the-art BLEU score of 41.8 after training for 3.5 days on eight GPUs, a small fraction of the training costs of the best models from the literature. We show that the Transformerterm·ai generalizes well to other tasks by applying it successfully to English constituency parsing both with large and limited training data.

Introduction

Recurrent neural networks, long short-term memory [13] and gated recurrent [7] neural networks in particular, have been firmly established as state of the art approaches in sequenceterm·key modeling and transduction problems such as language modeling and machine translationterm·key [35, 2, 5]. Numerous efforts have since continued to push the boundaries of recurrent language models and encoderterm·key-decoderterm·key architectures [38, 24, 15].

Recurrent models typically factor computation along the symbol positions of the input and output sequences. Aligning the positions to steps in computation time, they generate a sequenceterm·key of hidden states ht, as a function of the previous hidden state ht−1 and the input for position t. This inherently sequential nature precludes parallelization within training examples, which becomes critical at longer sequenceterm·key lengths, as memory constraints limit batching across examples. Recent work has achieved significant improvements in computational efficiency through factorization tricks [21] and conditional computation [32], while also improving model performance in case of the latter. The fundamental constraint of sequential computation, however, remains.

Attentionterm·key mechanisms have become an integral part of compelling sequenceterm·key modeling and transduc- tion models in various tasks, allowing modeling of dependencies without regard to their distance in the input or output sequences [2, 19]. In all but a few cases [27], however, such attentionterm·key mechanisms are used in conjunction with a recurrent network.

In this work we propose the Transformerterm·ai, a model architecture eschewing recurrenceterm·key and instead relying entirely on an attentionterm·key mechanism to draw global dependencies between input and output. The Transformerterm·ai allows for significantly more parallelization and can reach a new state of the art in translationterm·key quality after being trained for as little as twelve hours on eight P100 GPUs.

2 Background

The goal of reducing sequential computation also forms the foundation of the Extended Neural GPU [16], ByteNet [18] and ConvS2S [9], all of which use convolutional neural networks as basic building block, computing hidden representations in parallel for all input and output positions. In these models, the number of operations required to relate signals from two arbitrary input or output positions grows in the distance between positions, linearly for ConvS2S and logarithmically for ByteNet. This makes it more difficult to learn dependencies between distant positions [12]. In the Transformerterm·ai this is reduced to a constant number of operations, albeit at the cost of reduced effective resolution due to averaging attentionterm·key-weighted positions, an effect we counteract with Multi-Head Attentionterm·ai as described in section 3.2.

Self-attentionterm·ai, sometimes called intra-attentionterm·key is an attentionterm·key mechanism relating different positions of a single sequenceterm·key in order to compute a representation of the sequenceterm·key. Self-attentionterm·ai has been used successfully in a variety of tasks including reading comprehension, abstractive summarization, textual entailment and learning task-independent sentence representations [4, 27, 28, 22].

End-to-end memory networks are based on a recurrent attentionterm·key mechanism instead of sequenceterm·key- aligned recurrenceterm·key and have been shown to perform well on simple-language question answering and language modeling tasks [34].

To the best of our knowledge, however, the Transformerterm·ai is the first transduction model relying entirely on self-attentionterm·ai to compute representations of its input and output without using sequenceterm·key- aligned RNNs or convolution. In the following sections, we will describe the Transformerterm·ai, motivate self-attentionterm·ai and discuss its advantages over models such as [17, 18] and [9].

3 Model Architecture

Most competitive neural sequenceterm·key transduction models have an encoderterm·key-decoderterm·key structure [5, 2, 35]. Here, the encoderterm·key maps an input sequenceterm·key of symbol representations (x1, ..., xn) to a sequenceterm·key of continuous representations z = (z1, ..., zn). Given z, the decoderterm·key then generates an output sequenceterm·key (y1, ..., ym) of symbols one element at a time. At each step the model is auto-regressive [10], consuming the previously generated symbols as additional input when generating the next.

The Transformerterm·ai follows this overall architecture using stacked self-attentionterm·ai and point-wise, fully connected layers for both the encoderterm·key and decoderterm·key, shown in the left and right halves of Figure 1, respectively.

3.1 Encoderterm·key and Decoderterm·key Stacks

Encoderterm·key: The encoderterm·key is composed of a stack of N = 6 identical layers. Each layer has two sub-layers. The first is a multi-head self-attentionterm·ai mechanism, and the second is a simple, position- wise fully connected feed-forward network. We employ a residual connection [11] around each of the two sub-layers, followed by layer normalization [1]. That is, the output of each sub-layer is LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x)), where Sublayer(x) is the function implemented by the sub-layer itself. To facilitate these residual connections, all sub-layers in the model, as well as the embedding layers, produce outputs of dimension dmodel = 512.

Decoderterm·key: The decoderterm·key is also composed of a stack of N = 6 identical layers. In addition to the two sub-layers in each encoderterm·key layer, the decoderterm·key inserts a third sub-layer, which performs multi-head attentionterm·ai over the output of the encoderterm·key stack. Similar to the encoderterm·key, we employ residual connections around each of the sub-layers, followed by layer normalization. We also modify the self-attentionterm·ai sub-layer in the decoderterm·key stack to prevent positions from attending to subsequent positions. This masking, combined with fact that the output embeddings are offset by one position, ensures that the predictions for position i can depend only on the known outputs at positions less than i.

3.2.3 Applications of Attentionterm·key in our Model

The Transformerterm·ai uses multi-head attentionterm·ai in three different ways:

• In "encoderterm·key-decoderterm·key attentionterm·key" layers, the queries come from the previous decoderterm·key layer, and the memory keys and values come from the output of the encoderterm·key. This allows every position in the decoderterm·key to attend over all positions in the input sequenceterm·key. This mimics the typical encoderterm·key-decoderterm·key attentionterm·key mechanisms in sequenceterm·key-to-sequenceterm·key models such as [38, 2, 9].

• The encoderterm·key contains self-attentionterm·ai layers. In a self-attentionterm·ai layer all of the keys, values and queries come from the same place, in this case, the output of the previous layer in the encoderterm·key. Each position in the encoderterm·key can attend to all positions in the previous layer of the encoderterm·key.

• Similarly, self-attentionterm·ai layers in the decoderterm·key allow each position in the decoderterm·key to attend to all positions in the decoderterm·key up to and including that position. We need to prevent leftward information flow in the decoderterm·key to preserve the auto-regressive property. We implement this inside of scaled dot-product attentionterm·key by masking out (setting to −∞) all values in the input of the softmax which correspond to illegal connections. See Figure 2.

3.5 Positional Encodingterm·ai

Since our model contains no recurrenceterm·key and no convolution, in order for the model to make use of the order of the sequenceterm·key, we must inject some information about the relative or absolute position of the tokens in the sequenceterm·key. To this end, we add "positional encodings" to the input embeddings at the bottoms of the encoderterm·key and decoderterm·key stacks. The positional encodings have the same dimension dmodel as the embeddings, so that the two can be summed. There are many choices of positional encodings, learned and fixed [9].

In this work, we use sine and cosine functions of different frequencies:

P E(pos,2i) = sin(pos/100002i/dmodel) P E(pos,2i+1) = cos(pos/100002i/dmodel)

where pos is the position and i is the dimension. That is, each dimension of the positional encodingterm·ai corresponds to a sinusoid. The wavelengths form a geometric progression from 2π to 10000 · 2π. We chose this function because we hypothesized it would allow the model to easily learn to attend by relative positions, since for any fixed offset k, P Epos+k can be represented as a linear function of P Epos.

We also experimented with using learned positional embeddings [9] instead, and found that the two versions produced nearly identical results (see Table 3 row (E)). We chose the sinusoidal version because it may allow the model to extrapolate to sequenceterm·key lengths longer than the ones encountered during training.

4 Why Self-Attentionterm·ai

In this section we compare various aspects of self-attentionterm·ai layers to the recurrent and convolu- tional layers commonly used for mapping one variable-length sequenceterm·key of symbol representations (x1, ..., xn) to another sequenceterm·key of equal length (z1, ..., zn), with xi, zi ∈ Rd, such as a hidden layer in a typical sequenceterm·key transduction encoderterm·key or decoderterm·key. Motivating our use of self-attentionterm·ai we consider three desiderata.

One is the total computational complexity per layer. Another is the amount of computation that can be parallelized, as measured by the minimum number of sequential operations required.

The third is the path length between long-range dependencies in the network. Learning long-range dependencies is a key challenge in many sequenceterm·key transduction tasks. One key factor affecting the ability to learn such dependencies is the length of the paths forward and backward signals have to traverse in the network. The shorter these paths between any combination of positions in the input and output sequences, the easier it is to learn long-range dependencies [12]. Hence we also compare the maximum path length between any two input and output positions in networks composed of the different layer types.

As noted in Table 1, a self-attentionterm·ai layer connects all positions with a constant number of sequentially executed operations, whereas a recurrent layer requires O(n) sequential operations. In terms of computational complexity, self-attentionterm·ai layers are faster than recurrent layers when the sequenceterm·key

length n is smaller than the representation dimensionality d, which is most often the case with sentence representations used by state-of-the-art models in machine translations, such as word-piece [38] and byte-pair [31] representations. To improve computational performance for tasks involving very long sequences, self-attentionterm·ai could be restricted to considering only a neighborhood of size r in the input sequenceterm·key centered around the respective output position. This would increase the maximum path length to O(n/r). We plan to investigate this approach further in future work.

A single convolutional layer with kernel width k < n does not connect all pairs of input and output positions. Doing so requires a stack of O(n/k) convolutional layers in the case of contiguous kernels, or O(logk(n)) in the case of dilated convolutionsterm·key [18], increasing the length of the longest paths between any two positions in the network. Convolutional layers are generally more expensive than recurrent layers, by a factor of k. Separable convolutionsterm·key [6], however, decrease the complexity considerably, to O(k · n · d + n · d2). Even with k = n, however, the complexity of a separable convolution is equal to the combination of a self-attentionterm·ai layer and a point-wise feed-forward layer, the approach we take in our model.

As side benefit, self-attentionterm·ai could yield more interpretable models. We inspect attentionterm·key distributions from our models and present and discuss examples in the appendix. Not only do individual attentionterm·key heads clearly learn to perform different tasks, many appear to exhibit behavior related to the syntactic and semantic structure of the sentences.

5 Training

This section describes the training regime for our models.

5.1 Training Data and Batching

We trained on the standard WMT 2014 English-German dataset consisting of about 4.5 million sentence pairs. Sentences were encoded using byte-pair encoding [3], which has a shared source- target vocabulary of about 37000 tokens. For English-French, we used the significantly larger WMT 2014 English-French dataset consisting of 36M sentences and split tokens into a 32000 word-piece vocabulary [38]. Sentence pairs were batched together by approximate sequenceterm·key length. Each training batch contained a set of sentence pairs containing approximately 25000 source tokens and 25000 target tokens.

5.2 Hardware and Schedule

We trained our models on one machine with 8 NVIDIA P100 GPUs. For our base models using the hyperparameters described throughout the paper, each training step took about 0.4 seconds. We trained the base models for a total of 100,000 steps or 12 hours. For our big models,(described on the bottom line of table 3), step time was 1.0 seconds. The big models were trained for 300,000 steps (3.5 days).

5.3 Optimizer

We used the Adam optimizer [20] with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.98 and ε = 10−9. We varied the learning rate over the course of training, according to the formula:

6 Results

6.1 Machine Translationterm·key

On the WMT 2014 English-to-German translationterm·key task, the big transformerterm·ai model (Transformerterm·ai (big) in Table 2) outperforms the best previously reported models (including ensembles) by more than 2.0 BLEU, establishing a new state-of-the-art BLEU score of 28.4. The configuration of this model is listed in the bottom line of Table 3. Training took 3.5 days on 8 P100 GPUs. Even our base model surpasses all previously published models and ensembles, at a fraction of the training cost of any of the competitive models.

On the WMT 2014 English-to-French translationterm·key task, our big model achieves a BLEU score of 41.0, outperforming all of the previously published single models, at less than 1/4 the training cost of the previous state-of-the-art model. The Transformerterm·ai (big) model trained for English-to-French used dropout rate Pdrop = 0.1, instead of 0.3.

For the base models, we used a single model obtained by averaging the last 5 checkpoints, which were written at 10-minute intervals. For the big models, we averaged the last 20 checkpoints. We used beam search with a beam size of 4 and length penalty α = 0.6 [38]. These hyperparameters were chosen after experimentation on the development set. We set the maximum output length during inference to input length + 50, but terminate early when possible [38].

7 Conclusion

In this work, we presented the Transformerterm·ai, the first sequenceterm·key transduction model based entirely on attentionterm·key, replacing the recurrent layers most commonly used in encoderterm·key-decoderterm·key architectures with multi-headed self-attentionterm·ai.

For translationterm·key tasks, the Transformerterm·ai can be trained significantly faster than architectures based on recurrent or convolutional layers. On both WMT 2014 English-to-German and WMT 2014 English-to-French translationterm·key tasks, we achieve a new state of the art. In the former task our best model outperforms even all previously reported ensembles.

We are excited about the future of attentionterm·key-based models and plan to apply them to other tasks. We plan to extend the Transformerterm·ai to problems involving input and output modalities other than text and to investigate local, restricted attentionterm·key mechanisms to efficiently handle large inputs and outputs such as images, audio and video. Making generation less sequential is another research goals of ours.
