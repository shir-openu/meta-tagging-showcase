Introduction

Many questions in medical research are investigated in observationalterm·key studiesterm·key [1]. Much of the research into the cause of diseases relies on cohort, case-control, or cross-sectional studiesterm·key. Observationalterm·key studiesterm·key also have a role in research into the benefits and harms of medical interventions [2]. Rando- mised trials cannot answer all important questions about a given intervention. For example, observationalterm·key studiesterm·key are more suitable to detect rare or late adverse effects of treatments, and are more likely to provide an indication of what is achieved in daily medical practice [3].

Research should be reported transparently so that readers can follow what was planned, what was done, what was found, and what conclusions were drawn. The credibility of research depends on a critical assessment by others of the strengths and weaknesses in study design, conduct, and analysis. Transparentterm·key reportingterm·key is also needed to judge whether and how results can be included in systematic reviews [4,5]. An analysis of epidemiological studiesterm·key published in general medical and specialist journals found that the rationale behind the choice of potential confounding variables was often not reported [6]. In a surveyterm·mth of longitudinal studiesterm·key in stroke research, 17 of 49 articles (35%) did not specify the eligibility criteria [8]. Recommendationsterm·key on the reportingterm·key of research can improve reportingterm·key quality. Implications and Limitations

The STROBEterm·key Statement was developed to assist authors when writing up analytical observationalterm·key studiesterm·key, to support editors and reviewers when considering such articles for publication, and to help readers when critically appraising published articles. We developed the checklistterm·key through an open process, taking into account the experience gained with previous initiatives, in particular CONSORT. We reviewed the relevant empirical evidence as well as methodological work, and subjected consecutive drafts to an extensive iterative process of consultation. The checklistterm·key presented here is thus based on input from a large number of individuals with diverse backgrounds and perspectives. The comprehensive explanatory article [18–20], which is intended for use alongside the checklistterm·key, also benefited greatly from this consultation process.

Observationalterm·key studiesterm·key serve a wide range of purposes, on a continuum from the discovery of new findings to the confirmation or refutation of previous findings [18–20]. Some studiesterm·key are essentially exploratory and raise interesting hypotheses. Others pursue clearly defined hypotheses in available data. In yet another type of studiesterm·key, the collection of new data is planned carefully on the basis of an existing hypothesis. We believe the present checklistterm·key can be useful for all these studiesterm·key, since the readers always need to know what was planned (and what was not), what was done, what was found, and what the results mean. We acknowledge that STROBEterm·key is currently limited to three main observational study designsterm·def. We would welcome extensions that adapt the checklistterm·key to other designs—e.g., case-crossover studiesterm·key or ecological studiesterm·key—and also to specific topic areas. Four extensions are now available for the CONSORT statement [21–24]. A first extension to STROBEterm·key is underway for gene- disease association studiesterm·key: the STROBEterm·key Extension to Genetic Association studiesterm·key (STREGA) initiative [25]. We ask those who aim to develop extensions of the STROBEterm·key Statement to contact the coordinating group first to avoid duplication of effort.

The STROBEterm·key Statement should not be interpreted as an attempt to prescribe the reportingterm·key of observationalterm·key research in a rigid format. The checklistterm·key itemsterm·key should be addressed in sufficient detail and with clarity somewhere in an article, but the order and format for presenting information depends on author preferences, journal style, and the traditions of the research field. For instance, we discuss the reportingterm·key of results under a number of separate itemsterm·key, while recognizing that authors might address several itemsterm·key within a single section of text or in a table. Also, item 22, on the source of funding and the role of funders, could be addressed in an

Authors of randomised clinical trials were asked by an editor of a specialist medical journal to ‘‘CONSORT’’ their manuscripts on submission [26]. We believe that manuscripts should not be ‘‘STROBEd’’, in the sense of regulating style or terminology. We encourage authors to use narrative elements, including the description of illustrative cases, to complement the essential information about their study, and to make their articles an interesting read [27].

We emphasise that the STROBEterm·key Statement was not developed as a tool for assessing the quality of published observationalterm·key research. Such instruments have been devel- oped by other groups and were the subject of a recent systematic review [28]. In the Explanation and Elaboration paper, we used several examples of good reportingterm·key from studiesterm·key whose results were not confirmed in further research – the important feature was the good reportingterm·key, not whether the research was of good quality. However, if STROBEterm·key is adopted by authors and journals, issues such as confounding, bias, and generalisability could become more transparentterm·key, which might help temper the over-enthusiastic reportingterm·key of new findings in the scientific community and popular media [29], and improve the methodology of studiesterm·key in the long term. Better reportingterm·key may also help to have more informed decisions about when new studiesterm·key are needed, and what they should address.

We did not undertake a comprehensive systematic review for each of the checklistterm·key itemsterm·key and sub-itemsterm·key, or do our own research to fill gaps in the evidence base. Further, although no one was excluded from the process, the composition of the group of contributors was influenced by existing networks and was not representative in terms of geography (it was dominated by contributors from Europe and North America) and probably was not representative in terms of research interests and disciplines. We stress that STROBEterm·key and other recommendationsterm·key on the reportingterm·key of research should be seen as evolving documents that require continual assess- ment, refinement, and, if necessary, change. We welcome suggestions for the further dissemination of STROBEterm·key—e.g., by re-publication of the present article in specialist journals and in journals published in other languages. Groups or individuals who intend to translate the checklistterm·key to other languages should consult the coordinating group beforehand. We will revise the checklistterm·key in the future, taking into account comments, criticism, new evidence, and experience from its use. We invite readers to submit their comments via the STROBEterm·key Web site (http://www.strobeterm·key-statement.org/).
