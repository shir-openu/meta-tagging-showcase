ABSTRACT Despite widespread adoption, machine learningterm·ai models re- main mostly black boxes. Understanding the reasons behind predictions is, however, quite important in assessing trustterm·key, which is fundamental if one plans to take action based on a predictionterm·key, or when choosing whether to deploy a new modelterm·key. Such understanding also provides insights into the modelterm·key, which can be used to transform an untrustworthy modelterm·key or predictionterm·key into a trustworthy one.

In this work, we propose LIMEterm·key, a novel explanationterm·key tech- nique that explains the predictions of any classifierterm·key in an in- terpretable and faithfulterm·key manner, by learning an interpretableterm·key modelterm·key locally around the predictionterm·key. We also propose a method to explain models by presenting representative indi- vidual predictions and their explanations in a non-redundant way, framing the task as a submodular optimization prob- lem. We demonstrate the flexibility of these methods by explaining different models for text (e.g. random forests) and image classification (e.g. neural networks). We show the utility of explanations via novel experiments, both simulated and with human subjects, on various scenarios that require trustterm·key: deciding if one should trustterm·key a predictionterm·key, choosing between models, improving an untrustworthy classifierterm·key, and identifying why a classifierterm·key should not be trusted.

Determining trustterm·key in individual predictions is an important problem when the modelterm·key is used for decision making. When using machine learningterm·ai for medical diagnosis [6] or terrorism detection, for example, predictions cannot be acted upon on blind faith, as the consequences may be catastrophic.

Every machine learningterm·ai application also requires a certain measure of overall trustterm·key in the modelterm·key. Development and evaluation of a classification modelterm·key often consists of collect- ing annotated data, of which a held-out subset is used for automated evaluation. There are several ways a modelterm·key or its evaluation can go wrong. Data leakage, for example, defined as the uninten- tional leakage of signal into the training (and validation) data that would not appear when deployed [14], potentially increases accuracy. This issue would be incredibly challenging to identify just by observing the predictions and the raw data, but much easier if explanations such as the one in Figure 1 are provided, as patient ID would be listed as an explanationterm·key for predictions. Another particularly hard to detect problem is dataset shift [5], where training data is different than test data (we give an example in the famous 20 newsgroups dataset later on). The insights given by expla- nations are particularly helpful in identifying what must be done to convert an untrustworthy modelterm·key into a trustworthy one – for example, removing leaked data or changing the training data to avoid dataset shift.

An essential criterion for explanations is that they must be interpretableterm·key, i.e., provide qualitative understanding between the input variables and the response. We note that interpretabilityterm·mth must take into account the user’s limitations. Thus, a linear modelterm·key [24], a gradient vector [2] or an additive modelterm·key [6] may or may not be interpretableterm·key. For example, if

Another essential criterion is local fidelity. Although it is often impossible for an explanationterm·key to be completely faithfulterm·key unless it is the complete description of the modelterm·key itself, for an explanationterm·key to be meaningful it must at least be locally faithfulterm·key, i.e. it must correspond to how the modelterm·key behaves in the vicinity of the instance being predicted. We note that local fidelity does not imply global fidelity: featuresterm·key that are globally important may not be important in the local context, and vice versa. While global fidelity would imply local fidelity, identifying globally faithfulterm·key explanations that are interpretableterm·key remains a challenge for complex models.

While there are models that are inherently interpretableterm·key [6, 17, 26, 27], an explainer should be able to explain any modelterm·key, and thus be modelterm·key-agnostic (i.e. treat the original modelterm·key as a black box). Apart from the fact that many state-of- the-art classifiers are not currently interpretableterm·key, this also provides flexibility to explain future classifiers.

In addition to explaining predictions, providing a global perspective is important to ascertain trustterm·key in the modelterm·key. As mentioned before, accuracy may often not be a suitable metric to evaluate the modelterm·key, and thus we want to explain the modelterm·key. Building upon the explanations for individual predictions, we select a few explanations to present to the user, such that they are representative of the modelterm·key.

MODELterm·key-AGNOSTIC EXPLANATIONS We now present Local Interpretableterm·key Modelterm·key-agnostic Expla- nations (LIMEterm·key). The overall goal of LIMEterm·key is to identify an interpretableterm·key modelterm·key over the interpretableterm·key representation that is locally faithfulterm·key to the classifierterm·key.

3.1 Interpretableterm·key Data Representations

Before we present the explanationterm·key system, it is impor- tant to distinguish between featuresterm·key and interpretableterm·key data representations. As mentioned before, interpretableterm·key expla- nations need to use a representation that is understandable to humans, regardless of the actual featuresterm·key used by the modelterm·key. For example, a possible interpretableterm·key representation for text classification is a binary vector indicating the pres- ence or absence of a word, even though the classifierterm·key may use more complex (and incomprehensible) featuresterm·key such as word embeddings. Likewise for image classification, an in- terpretable representation may be a binary vector indicating the “presence” or “absence” of a contiguous patch of similar pixels (a super-pixel), while the classifierterm·key may represent the image as a tensor with three color channels per pixel. We denote x ∈ Rd be the original representation of an instance being explained, and we use x(cid:48) ∈ {0, 1}d(cid:48) to denote a binary vector for its interpretableterm·key representation.

3.2 Fidelity-Interpretabilityterm·mth Trade-off

Formally, we define an explanationterm·key as a modelterm·key g ∈ G, where G is a class of potentially interpretableterm·key models, such as linear models, decision trees, or falling rule lists [27], i.e. a modelterm·key g ∈ G can be readily presented to the user with visual or textual artifacts. The domain of g is {0, 1}d(cid:48) , i.e. g acts over absence/presence of the interpretableterm·key components. As not every g ∈ G may be simple enough to be interpretableterm·key - thus we let Ω(g) be a measure of complexity (as opposed to interpretabilityterm·mth) of the explanationterm·key g ∈ G. For example, for decision trees Ω(g) may be the depth of the tree, while for linear models, Ω(g) may be the number of non-zero weights. Let the modelterm·key being explained be denoted f : Rd → R. In classification, f (x) is the probability (or a binary indicator) that x belongs to a certain class1. We further use πx(z) as a proximity measure between an instance z to x, so as to define locality around x. Finally, let L(f, g, πx) be a measure of how unfaithful g is in approximating f in the locality defined by πx. In order to ensure both interpretabilityterm·mth and local fidelity, we must minimize L(f, g, πx) while having Ω(g) be low enough to be interpretableterm·key by humans. 3.3 Sampling for Local Exploration

We want to minimize the locality-aware loss L(f, g, πx) without making any assumptions about f , since we want the explainer to be modelterm·key-agnostic. We sample instances around x(cid:48) by drawing nonzero elements of x(cid:48) uniformly at random (where the number of such draws is also uniformly sampled). 4. SUBMODULAR PICK FOR EXPLAINING MODELS

Although an explanationterm·key of a single predictionterm·key provides some understanding into the reliability of the classifierterm·key to the user, it is not sufficient to evaluate and assess trustterm·key in the modelterm·key as a whole. We propose to give a global understanding of the modelterm·key by explaining a set of individual instances. This approach is still modelterm·key agnostic, and is complementary to computing summary statistics such as held-out accuracy.

Even though explanations of multiple instances can be insightful, these instances need to be selected judiciously, since users may not have the time to examine a large number of explanations. We represent the time/patience that humans have by a budget B that denotes the number of explanations they are willing to look at in order to understand a modelterm·key. Given a set of instances X, we define the pick step as the task of selecting B instances for the user to inspect.

The pick step is not dependent on the existence of explana- tions - one of the main purpose of tools like Modeltracker [1] and others [11] is to assist users in selecting instances them- selves, and examining the raw data and predictions. However, since looking at raw data is not enough to understand predic- tions and get insights, the pick step should take into account the explanations that accompany each predictionterm·key. Moreover, this method should pick a diverse, representative set of expla- nations to show the user – i.e. non-redundant explanations that represent how the modelterm·key behaves globally.

5. SIMULATED USER EXPERIMENTS

In this section, we present simulated user experiments to evaluate the utility of explanations in trustterm·key-related tasks. In particular, we address the following questions: (1) Are the explanations faithfulterm·key to the modelterm·key, (2) Can the explanations aid users in ascertaining trustterm·key in predictions, and (3) Are the explanations useful for evaluating the modelterm·key as a whole. Code and data for replicating our experiments are available at https://github.com/marcotcr/limeterm·key-experiments.

5.1 Experimentterm·mth Setup

We use two sentiment analysis datasets (books and DVDs, 2000 instances each) where the task is to classify prod- uct reviews as positive or negative [4]. We train decision trees (DT), logistic regression with L2 regularization (LR), nearest neighbors (NN), and support vector machines with RBF kernel (SVM), all using bag of words as featuresterm·key. We also include random forests (with 1000 trees) trained with the average word2vec embedding [19] (RF), a modelterm·key that is impossible to interpret without a technique like LIMEterm·key. We use the implementations and default parameters of scikit- learn, unless noted otherwise. We divide each dataset into train (1600 instances) and test (400 instances).

To explain individual predictions, we compare our pro- posed approach (LIMEterm·key), with parzen [2], a method that approximates the black box classifierterm·key globally with Parzen windows, and explains individual predictions by taking the gradient of the predictionterm·key probability function. For parzen, we take the K featuresterm·key with the highest absolute gradients as explanations. We set the hyper-parameters for parzen and LIMEterm·key using cross validation, and set N = 15, 000. We also compare against a greedy procedure (similar to Martens and Provost [18]) in which we greedily remove featuresterm·key that contribute the most to the predicted class until the predictionterm·key changes (or we reach the maximum of K featuresterm·key), and a random procedure that randomly picks K featuresterm·key as an explanationterm·key. We set K to 10 for our experiments.

For experiments where the pick procedure applies, we either do random selection (random pick, RP) or the procedure described in §4 (submodular pick, SP). We refer to pick- explainer combinations by adding RP or SP as a prefix.

5.4 Can I trustterm·key this modelterm·key?

In the final simulated user experimentterm·mth, we evaluate whether the explanations can be used for modelterm·key selection, simulating the case where a human has to decide between two competing models with similar accuracy on validation data. For this purpose, we add 10 artificially “noisy” featuresterm·key. Specifically, on training and validation sets (80/20 split of the original training data), each artificial feature appears in 10% of the examples in one class, and 20% of the other, while on the test instances, each artificial feature appears in 10% of the examples in each class. This recreates the situation where the models use not only featuresterm·key that are informative in the real world, but also ones that introduce spurious correlations. We create pairs of competing classifiers by repeatedly training pairs of random forests with 30 trees until their validation accuracy is within 0.1% of each other, but their test accuracy differs by at least 5%. Thus, it is not possible to identify the better classifierterm·key (the one with higher test accuracy) from the accuracy on the validation data.

The goal of this experimentterm·mth is to evaluate whether a user can identify the better classifierterm·key based on the explanations of B instances from the validation set. The simulated human marks the set of artificial featuresterm·key that appear in the B explanations as untrustworthy, following which we evaluate how many total predictions in the validation set should be trusted (as in the previous section, treating only marked featuresterm·key as untrustworthy). Then, we select the classifierterm·key with

fewer untrustworthy predictions, and compare this choice to the classifierterm·key with higher held-out test set accuracy.

We present the accuracy of picking the correct classifierterm·key as B varies, averaged over 800 runs, in Figure 8. LIMEterm·key is consistently better than greedy, irre- spective of the pick method. Further, combining submodular pick with LIMEterm·key outperforms all other methods, in particular it is much better than RP-LIMEterm·key when only a few examples are shown to the users. For experiments in §6.2 and §6.3, we use the “Christianity” and “Atheism” documents from the 20 newsgroups dataset mentioned beforehand. Here we focus on general, modelterm·key-agnostic explanations that can be applied to any classifierterm·key or regressor that is appropriate for the domain - even ones that are yet to be proposed.

A common approach to model-agnostic explanationterm·def is learn- ing a potentially interpretableterm·key modelterm·key on the predictions of the original modelterm·key [2, 7, 22]. Having the explanationterm·key be a gradient vector [2] captures a similar locality intuition to that of LIMEterm·key. However, interpreting the coefficients on the gradient is difficult, particularly for confident predictions (where gradient is near zero). Further, these explanations ap- proximate the original modelterm·key globally, thus maintaining local fidelity becomes a significant challenge, as our experiments demonstrate. In contrast, LIMEterm·key solves the much more feasi- ble task of finding a modelterm·key that approximates the original modelterm·key locally. The idea of perturbing inputs for explanations has been explored before [24], where the authors focus on learning a specific contribution modelterm·key, as opposed to our general framework. None of these approaches explicitly take cognitive limitations into account, and thus may produce non-interpretableterm·key explanations, such as a gradients or linear models with thousands of non-zero weights. The problem becomes worse if the original featuresterm·key are nonsensical to humans (e.g. word embeddings). In contrast, LIMEterm·key incor- porates interpretabilityterm·mth both in the optimization and in our notion of interpretableterm·key representation, such that domain and task specific interpretabilityterm·mth criteria can be accommodated.

7. RELATED WORK

The problems with relying on validation set accuracy as the primary measure of trustterm·key have been well studied. Practi- tioners consistently overestimate their modelterm·key’s accuracy [20], propagate feedback loops [23], or fail to notice data leaks [14]. In order to address these issues, researchers have proposed tools like Gestalt [21] and Modeltracker [1], which help users navigate individual instances. These tools are complemen- tary to LIMEterm·key in terms of explaining models, since they do not address the problem of explaining individual predictions. Further, our submodular pick procedure can be incorporated in such tools to aid users in navigating larger datasets.

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK In this paper, we argued that trustterm·key is crucial for effective human interaction with machine learningterm·ai systems, and that explaining individual predictions is important in assessing trustterm·key. We proposed LIMEterm·key, a modular and extensible ap- proach to faithfully explain the predictions of any modelterm·key in an interpretableterm·key manner. We also introduced SP-LIMEterm·key, a method to select representative and non-redundant predic- tions, providing a global view of the modelterm·key to users. Our experiments demonstrated that explanations are useful for a variety of models in trustterm·key-related tasks in the text and image domains, with both expert and non-expert users: deciding between models, assessing trustterm·key, improving untrustworthy models, and getting insights into predictions.

There are a number of avenues of future work that we would like to explore. Although we describe only sparse linear models as explanations, our framework supports the exploration of a variety of explanationterm·key families, such as de- cision trees; it would be interesting to see a comparative study on these with real users. One issue that we do not mention in this work was how to perform the pick step for images, and we would like to address this limitation in the future. The domain and modelterm·key agnosticism enables us to explore a variety of applications, and we would like to inves- tigate potential uses in speech, video, and medical domains, as well as recommendation systems. Finally, we would like to explore theoretical properties (such as the appropriate number of samples) and computational optimizations (such as using parallelization and GPU processing), in order to provide the accurate, real-time explanations that are critical for any human-in-the-loop machine learningterm·ai system.
