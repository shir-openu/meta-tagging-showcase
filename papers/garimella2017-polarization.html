Abstract

Social mediaterm·dom has played an important role in shaping politicalterm·key discourse over the last decade. At the same time, it is often perceived to have increased political polarizationterm·dom, thanks to the scale of discussions and their public nature. In this paper, we try to answer the question of whether po- litical polarizationterm·key in the US on Twitterterm·key has increased over the last eight years. We analyze a large longitudinal Twitterterm·key dataset of 679,000 usersterm·key and look at signs of polarizationterm·key in their (i) networkterm·key - how people follow politicalterm·key and media ac- counts, (ii) tweeting behavior - whether they retweetterm·key contentterm·key from both sides, and (iii) contentterm·key - how partisanterm·key the hashtagsterm·key they use are. Our analysis shows that online polarizationterm·key has indeed increased over the past eight years and that, depending on the measure, the relative change is 10%-20%. Our study is one of very few with such a long-term perspective, encom- passing two US presidential elections and two mid-term elec- tions, providing a rare longitudinal analysis.

Introduction Social mediaterm·dom has had a tremendous impact on society by re- defining ways in which we get exposed to information. A recent Pew survey found that more than 60% of Americans get their news from social mediaterm·dom [7]. While there have been a lot of benefits that social mediaterm·dom has brought about, includ- ing access to a wealth of knowledge, connections and infor- mation, social mediaterm·dom is also hypothesized to encourage the creation of echo chambers, where usersterm·key reinforce their own viewpoints and discredit the view points they do not agree with.1 This can potentially lead to a downward spiral of ever increasing politicalterm·key polarization2, which, in turn, makes it harder to have a fact-based debate and to reach a consensus on controversial issues.

Though a lot of studies have shown the existence of po- larization on social mediaterm·dom [4, 1], little analysis has been done on long term trends. Experiments In our quest to understand long-term polarizationterm·key trends, we look at three aspects: (i) If user are now more likely to follow usersterm·key across the politicalterm·key spectrum, (ii) if they are now more likely to retweetterm·key such usersterm·key, and (iii) if usersterm·key are now more likely to use hashtagsterm·key which are used by both sides. Here we describe the three types of experiments we performed, related to following and retweeting behavior in the first part, followed by experiments related to hashtagsterm·key usage.

Following and Retweeting Behavior To observe changes in the polarizationterm·key of the following (retweeting) behavior, we wanted to track changes in the probability to follow (retweetterm·key) accounts from both sides. As, due to sparsity, following (retweeting) only a single user from one of the two sides is not necessarily a strong signal

Before observing any evidence, we gave each follow- ing (retweeting) user a uniform prior probability to follow (retweetterm·key) seed usersterm·key from either side. Concretely, we used a beta distribution with a uniform prior (α = β = 1), where α measures the left leaning and β the right leaning.

Then every follow (retweetterm·key) to either side increases the count for that side by +1, basically simulating a repeated coin toss where we are studying the bias of the coin. As the beta distribution is the conjugate prior of the binomial dis- tribution, we might obtain something like α = 4, β = 2 for a (mostly) left leaning user. The mean of the beta distribu- tion, and hence the “leaning” l of the following (retweeting) user, is defined as l = α/(α + β), taking the leftness as the direction of the index. We defined the polarizationterm·key p as p = 2 · |0.5 − l|, giving a measure between 0.0 and 1.0 mea- suring the deviation from a balanced leaning.

For each politicalterm·key follower/retweeter and each year, this method gives us a value of polarizationterm·key. Figure 1 plots the distribution of the average polarizationterm·key and shows temporal shifts. Note that whereas we have the following information for both politicalterm·key and media seed accounts, we only have the retweeting usersterm·key for politicalterm·key seed. Regardless of whether us- ing politician or media outlet seed accounts, and regardless of whether using following or retweeting information, polar- ization has increased from 2009 to 2016 between 10-20% in relative terms.

Hashtag Polarizationterm·key The third type of polarizationterm·key we analyze relates to contentterm·key polarizationterm·key, more specifically to the polarizationterm·key of hash- tags used by usersterm·key. Conceptually, a society could be thought of as polarized if there are two opposing sides who speak dif- ferent languages, in that they differ completely in the words they choose to describe things. For example, one person’s “global warming” could be another person’s “climategate”. We operationalize this idea by applying the methodology previously used in [15].

In their methodology, for a given week, a user is assigned a leaning based on the politicalterm·key seed accounts they retweetterm·key during that week. Usersterm·key not retweeting seed accounts dur- ing the week do not contribute. Each hashtag h is then as- signed a leaning lh between 0.0=right and 1.0=left based on the leaning of the usersterm·key using the hashtagsterm·key in the given week. Differences in user volumes for the two sides are corrected for and smoothing is applied to deal with sparsity. For each hashtag h in a given week, its polarizationterm·key ph is then, as be- fore, defined as ph = 2 · |0.5 − l|. The values of ph are then averaged across all h used in a given week by retweeting usersterm·key. See [15] for details.

To further reduce noise due to low volumes, in particu- lar during the early years, we (i) ignored hashtagsterm·key used by fewer than five usersterm·key, and (ii) computed moving averages across five weeks. To look for drifts in the time series, we first tested for stationarity of the time series. An augmented Dickey-Fuller test found the time series to be non-stationary (p < 0.0001). Next, we computed the linear fit across time and tested the value of the non-zero slope for statistical sig- nificance using a t-test (p < 0.0001).

Figure 2 shows the temporal changes for the measure of hashtag polarizationterm·key together with the linear fit superim- posed. Similar to the following and retweeting polarizationterm·key, there is a relative increase of about 20% between 2009 and 2016. Due to the finer time scale, Figure 2 also suggests that the time around elections corresponds to local maxima in polarizationterm·key, whereas the time after elections corresponds to local minima. For the 2010 midterm elections, however, this observation does not hold, potentially due to noisier es- timates based on fewer active usersterm·key on Twitterterm·key.

Conclusions

There is conflicting evidence on whether social mediaterm·dom (i) actively increases offline polarizationterm·key through the formation of online echo chambers, (ii) merely reflects offline polar- ization [14], or (iii) helps to reduce offline polarizationterm·key by exposing usersterm·key to a more diverse set of opinions than they would find in their offline social networkterm·key [3].

Though our analysis does not directly settle this debate, it provides evidence that polarizationterm·key on Twitterterm·key has in- creased over the past eight years, potentially reflecting in- creases in offline polarizationterm·key as those observed in the US House of Representatives [2]. Furthermore, for three differ- ent methodologies, the relative size of the increase of polar- ization was found to be between 10% and 20%.

In our work, we did not explicitly attempt to detect “astro- turfing” and other types of automated tweets [13]. However, due to the longitudinal nature of our study, by the time of the data collection (2016/2017) Twitterterm·key will have had time to catch most cases of usersterm·key violating their terms of ser- vice, suspending their accounts. More organic efforts such as hashtag hijacking [8] could still affect our analysis, though these effects are arguably also part of the politicalterm·key landscape and should be included.

Given the running up to the 2016 US presidential elec- tions and concerns of a President Trump - famous for his Twitterterm·key politics - doing little to attempt to reduce polariza- tion, we speculate that the online polarizationterm·key will continue to increase in the foreseeable future.

Finally, as social mediaterm·dom is coming of age and soon teenagers will no longer remember a pre-social-media era, new opportunities for longitudinal studies arise. At the same time, technical challenges related to “most recent activity only” API limitations hinder such studies. Still, we expect and look forward to more long-term analysis such as ours in the future.
