We define the relevant informationterm·dom in a signalterm·key x ∈ X as being the in- formation that this signalterm·key provides about another signalterm·key y ∈ Y . Examples include the informationterm·key that face images provide about the names of the peo- ple portrayed, or the informationterm·key that speech sounds provide about the words spoken. Understanding the signalterm·key x requires more than just predicting y, it also requires specifying which features of X play a role in the prediction. We formalize this problem as that of finding a short code for X that preserves the maximum informationterm·key about Y . That is, we squeeze the informationterm·key that X provides about Y through a ‘bottleneckterm·key’ formed by a limited set of codewords ̃X. This constrained optimization problem can be seen as a generalization of rateterm·key distortionterm·key theory in which the distortionterm·key measure d(x, ̃x) emerges from the joint statistics of X and Y . This approach yields an exact set of self consistent equations for the coding rules X → ̃X and ̃X → Y . Solutions to these equations can be found by a convergent re–estimation method that generalizes the Blahut–Arimoto algorithm. Our variational principle pro- vides a surprisingly rich framework for discussing a variety of problems in signalterm·key processing and learning, as will be described in detail elsewhere.

1 Introduction

A fundamental problem in formalizing our intuitive ideas about informationterm·key is to provide a quantitative notion of “meaningful” or “relevantterm·key” informationterm·key. These issues were intentionally left out of informationterm·key theory in its original formulation by Shannon, who focused attention on the problem of transmit- ting informationterm·key rather than judging its value to the recipient. Correspond- ingly, informationterm·key theory has often been viewed as being strictly a theory of communication, and this view has become so accepted that many people consider statistical and informationterm·key theoretic principles as almost irrelevant for the question of meaning. In contrast, we argue here that informationterm·key the- ory, in particular lossy source compressionterm·key, provides a natural quantitative approach to the question of “relevant informationterm·dom.” Specifically, we formu- late a variational principle for the extraction or efficient representation of relevant informationterm·dom. In related work [1] we argue that this single informa- tion theoretic principle contains as special cases a wide variety of problems, including prediction, filtering, and learning in its various forms.

The problem of extracting a relevantterm·key summary of data, a compressed description that captures only the relevantterm·key or meaningful informationterm·key, is not well posed without a suitable definition of relevance. A typical example is that of speech compressionterm·key. One can consider lossless compressionterm·key, but in any compressionterm·key beyond the entropy of speech some components of the signalterm·key cannot be reconstructed. On the other hand, a transcript of the spoken words has much lower entropy (by orders of magnitude) than the acoustic waveform, which means that it is possible to compress (much) further without losing any informationterm·key about the words and their meaning.

The standard analysis of lossy source compressionterm·key is “rateterm·key distortionterm·key the- ory,” which characterizes the tradeoff between the rateterm·key, or signalterm·key represen- tation size, and the average distortionterm·key of the reconstructed signalterm·key. Rateterm·key distortionterm·key theory determines the level of inevitable expected distortionterm·key, D, given the desired informationterm·key rateterm·key, R, in terms of the rateterm·key distortionterm·key function R(D). The main problem with rateterm·key distortionterm·key theory is in the need to specify the distortionterm·key function first, which in turn determines the relevantterm·key features of the signalterm·key. Those features, however, are often not explicitly known and

an arbitrary choice of the distortionterm·key function is in fact an arbitrary feature selection.

In the speech example, we have at best very partial knowledge of what precise components of the signalterm·key are perceived by our (neural) speech recog- nition system. Those relevantterm·key components depend not only on the complex structure of the auditory nervous system, but also on the sounds and utter- ances to which we are exposed during our early life. It therefore is virtually impossible to come up with the “correct” distortionterm·key function for acoustic signals. The same type of difficulty exists in many similar problems, such as natural language processing, bioinformatics (for example, what features of protein sequences determine their structure) or neural coding (what informa- tion is encoded by spike trains and how). This is the fundamental problem of feature selection in pattern recognition. Rateterm·key distortionterm·key theory does not provide a full answer to this problem since the choice of the distortionterm·key func- tion, which determines the relevantterm·key features, is not part of the theory. It is, however, a step in the right direction.

A possible solution comes from the fact that in many interesting cases we have access to an additional variable that determines what is relevantterm·key. In the speech case it might be the transcription of the signalterm·key, if we are interested in the speech recognition problem, or it might be the speaker’s identity if speaker identification is our goal. For natural language processing, it might be the part of speech labels for words in grammar checking, but the dictionary senses of ambiguous words in informationterm·key retrieval. Similarly, for the protein folding problem we have a joint database of sequences and three dimensional structures, and for neural coding a simultaneous recording of sensory stimuli and neural responses defines implicitly the relevantterm·key variables in each domain. All of these problems have the same formal underlying structure: extract the informationterm·key from one variable that is relevantterm·key for the prediction of another one. The choice of additional variable determines the relevantterm·key components or features of the signalterm·key in each case.

In this short paper we formalize this intuitive idea using an informa- tion theoretic approach which extends elements of rateterm·key distortionterm·key theory. We derive self consistent equations and an iterative algorithm for finding representations of the signalterm·key that capture its relevantterm·key structure, and prove

convergence of this algorithm.

Further work

The most fascinating aspect of the information bottleneckterm·mth principle is that it provides a unified framework for different informationterm·key processing problems, including prediction, filtering and learning [1]. There are already several successful applications of this method to various “real” problems, such as semantic clustering of English words [6], document classification [5], neural coding, and spectral analysis.
