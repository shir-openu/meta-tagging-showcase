Abstract

We develop an algorithm that can detect pneumoniaterm·key from chestterm·key X-rays at a level ex- ceeding practicing radiologists. Our algo- rithm, CheXNetterm·key, is a 121-layer convolutional neural networkterm·ai trained on ChestX-ray14, cur- rently the largest publicly available chestterm·key X- ray dataset, containing over 100,000 frontal- view X-rayterm·key imagesterm·key with 14 diseases. Four practicing academic radiologists annotate a test set, on which we compare the perfor- mance of CheXNetterm·key to that of radiologists. We find that CheXNetterm·key exceeds average ra- diologist performance on the F1 metric. We extend CheXNetterm·key to detect all 14 diseases in ChestX-ray14 and achieve state of the art re- sults on all 14 diseases.

1. Introduction

More than 1 million adults are hospitalized with pneu- monia and around 50,000 die from the diseaseterm·key every year in the US alone (CDC, 2017). Chestterm·key X-rays are currently the best available method for diagnosing pneumoniaterm·key (WHO, 2001), playing a crucial role in clin- ical care (Franquet, 2001) and epidemiological studies (Cherian et al., 2005). However, detecting pneumo- nia in chestterm·key X-rays is a challenging task that relies on the availability of expert radiologists. In this work, we present a model that can automatically detect pneu- monia from chestterm·key X-rays at a level exceeding practicing radiologists.

2. CheXNetterm·key

2.1. Problem Formulation

The pneumoniaterm·key detection task is a binary classification problem, where the input is a frontal-view chestterm·key X- ray image X and the output is a binary label y ∈ {0, 1} indicating the absence or presence of pneumoniaterm·key

respectively. For a single example in the training set, we optimize the weighted binary cross entropy loss

L(X, y) = −w+ · y log p(Y = 1|X)

−w− · (1 − y) log p(Y = 0|X),

where p(Y = i|X) is the probability that the networkterm·key assigns to the label i, w+ = |N |/(|P |+|N |), and w− = |P |/(|P |+|N |) with |P | and |N | the number of positive cases and negative cases of pneumoniaterm·key in the training set respectively.

2.2. Model Architecture and Training

CheXNetterm·key is a 121-layer Dense Convolutional Net- work (DenseNetterm·key) (Huang et al., 2016) trained on the ChestX-ray 14 dataset. DenseNets improve flow of in- formation and gradients through the networkterm·key, making the optimization of very deep networks tractable. We replace the final fully connected layer with one that has a single output, after which we apply a sigmoid nonlinearity.

The weights of the networkterm·key are initialized with weights from a model pretrained on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). The networkterm·key is trained end-to-end using Adam with standard parameters (β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999) (Kingma & Ba, 2014). We train the model using mini- batches of size 16. We use an initial learning rate of 0.001 that is decayed by a factor of 10 each time the validation loss plateaus after an epoch, and pick the model with the lowest validation loss.

3. Data

3.1. Training

We use the ChestX-ray14 datasetterm·def released by Wang et al. (2017) which contains 112,120 frontal-view X-rayterm·key imagesterm·key of 30,805 unique patients. Wang et al. (2017) annotate each image with up to 14 different thoracic pathology labels using automatic extraction methods on radiology reports. We label imagesterm·key that have pneu- monia as one of the annotated pathologies as positive examples and label all other imagesterm·key as negative exam- ples. For the pneumoniaterm·key detection task, we randomly split the dataset into training (28744 patients, 98637 imagesterm·key), validation (1672 patients, 6351 imagesterm·key), and test (389 patients, 420 imagesterm·key). There is no patient overlap between the sets.

Before inputting the imagesterm·key into the networkterm·key, we downscale the imagesterm·key to 224×224 and normalize based on the mean and standard deviation of imagesterm·key in the ImageNet training set. We also augment the training data with random horizontal flipping.

4. CheXNetterm·key vs. Radiologistterm·key

Performance

4.1. Comparison

We assess the performance of both radiologists and CheXNetterm·key on the test set for the pneumoniaterm·key detection task. Recall that for each of the imagesterm·key in the test set, we have 4 labels from four practicing radiologists and 1 label from CheXNetterm·key. We compute the F1 score for each individual radiologistterm·key and for CheXNetterm·key against each of the other 4 labels as ground truth. We report the mean of the 4 resulting F1 scores for each radi- ologist and for CheXNetterm·key, along with the average F1 across the radiologists. We use the bootstrap to con- struct 95% bootstrap confidence intervals (CIs), cal- culating the average F1 score for both the radiologists and CheXNetterm·key on 10,000 bootstrap samples, sampled with replacement from the test set. We take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the F1 scores as the 95%

bootstrap CI. We find that CheXNetterm·key achieves an F1 score of 0.435 (95% CI 0.387, 0.481), higher than the radiologistterm·key average of 0.387 (95% CI 0.330, 0.442). Ta- ble 1 summarizes the performance of each radiologistterm·key and of CheXNetterm·key.

To determine whether CheXNetterm·key’s performance is sta- tistically significantly higher than radiologistterm·key perfor- mance, we also calculate the difference between the average F1 score of CheXNetterm·key and the average F1 score of the radiologists on the same bootstrap samples. If the 95% CI on the difference does not include zero, we conclude there was a significant difference between the F1 score of CheXNetterm·key and the F1 score of the ra- diologists. We find that the difference in F1 scores — 0.051 (95% CI 0.005, 0.084) — does not contain 0, and therefore conclude that the performance of CheXNetterm·key is statistically significantly higher than radiologistterm·key per- formance.

4.2. Limitations

7. Related Work

Recent advancements in deep learningterm·ai and large datasets have enabled algorithms to surpass the per- formance of medical professionals in a wide variety of medical imagingterm·dom tasks, including diabetic retinopathy detection (Gulshan et al., 2016), skin cancer classifica-

tion (Esteva et al., 2017), arrhythmia detection (Ra- jpurkar et al., 2017), and hemorrhage identification (Grewal et al., 2017).

Automated diagnosis from chestterm·key radiographs has re- ceived increasing attention with algorithms for pul- monary tuberculosis classification (Lakhani & Sun- daram, 2017) and lung nodule detection (Huang et al., 2017). Islam et al. (2017) studied the performance of various convolutional architectures on different ab- normalities using the publicly available OpenI dataset (Demner-Fushman et al., 2015). Wang et al. (2017) released ChestX-ray-14, an order of magnitude larger than previous datasets of its kind, and also bench- marked different convolutional neural networkterm·ai archi- tectures pre-trained on ImageNet. Recently Yao et al. (2017) exploited statistical dependencies between la- bels in order make more accurate predictions, outper- forming Wang et al. (2017) on 13 of 14 classes.

8. Conclusion

Pneumoniaterm·key accounts for a significant proportion of patient morbidity and mortality (Gon ̧calves-Pereira et al., 2013). Early diagnosis and treatment of pneu- monia is critical to preventing complications including death (Aydogdu et al., 2010). With approximately 2 billion procedures per year, chestterm·key X-rays are the most common imaging examination tool used in practice, critical for screening, diagnosis, and management of a variety of diseases including pneumoniaterm·key (Raoof et al., 2012). However, two thirds of the global population lacks access to radiology diagnostics, according to an estimate by the World Health Organization (Mollura et al., 2010). There is a shortage of experts who can in- terpret X-rays, even when imaging equipment is avail- able, leading to increased mortality from treatable dis- eases (Kesselman et al., 2016).

We develop an algorithm which detects pneumoniaterm·key from frontal-view chestterm·key X-rayterm·key imagesterm·key at a level ex- ceeding practicing radiologists. We also show that a simple extension of our algorithm to detect multi- ple diseases outperforms previous state of the art on ChestX-ray14, the largest publicly available chestterm·key X- ray dataset. With automation at the level of experts, we hope that this technology can improve healthcare delivery and increase access to medical imagingterm·dom ex- pertise in parts of the world where access to skilled radiologists is limited.
