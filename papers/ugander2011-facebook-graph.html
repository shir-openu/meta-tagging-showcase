Introduction

The emergence of online socialterm·key networking services over the past decade has revolutionized how socialterm·key scientists study the structure of human relationships [1]. As individuals bring their socialterm·key relations online, the focal point of the internet is evolving from being a networkterm·key of documents to being a networkterm·key of people, and previously invisible socialterm·key structures are being captured at tremendous scale and with unprecedented detail. In this work, we characterize the structure of the world’s largest online social networkterm·dom, Facebook, in an effort to advance the state of the art in the empirical study of socialterm·key networks.

In its simplest form, a social networkterm·dom contains individuals as vertices and edges as relationships between vertices [2]. This abstract view of human relationships, while certainly limited, has been very useful for characterizing socialterm·key relationships, with structural measures of this networkterm·key abstraction finding active application to the study of everything from bargaining power [3] to psychological health [4]. Moreover, socialterm·key networks have been observed to display a broad range of unifying structural properties, including homophily, clusteringterm·key, the small-world effectterm·dom, heterogeneous distributions of friends, and community structure [5, 6].

Quantitative analysis of these relationships requires individuals to explicitly detail their socialterm·key net- works. Historically, studies of socialterm·key networks were limited to hundreds of individuals as data on socialterm·key relationships was collected through painstakingly difficult means. Online socialterm·key networks allow us to in- crease the scale and accuracy of such studies dramatically because new social networkterm·dom data, mostly from online sources, map out our socialterm·key relationships at a nearly global scale. Prior studies of online socialterm·key networks include research on Twitter, Flickr, Yahoo! 360, Cyworld, Myspace, Orkut, and LiveJournal among others [7–11].

The trend within this line of research is to measure larger and larger representations of socialterm·key networks,

including networks derived from email [12], telephony [13], and instant messaging [14] traces. Two recent studies of Renren [15] and MSN messenger [14] included 42 million and 180 million individuals respectively. Networkterm·key completeness is especially important in the study of online socialterm·key networks because unlike traditional socialterm·key science research, the members of online socialterm·key networks are not controlled random samples, and instead should be considered biased samples. While the demographics of these networks have begun to approach the demographics of the global population at large [16], the most accurate representation of our socialterm·key relationships will include as many people as possible. We are not there yet, but in this paper we characterize the entire social networkterm·dom of active members of Facebook in May 2011, a networkterm·key then comprised of 721 million active usersterm·key. To our knowledge, this is the largest social networkterm·dom ever analyzed.

Facebook has naturally attracted the attention of researchers in the past. Some of this research has been devoted towards understanding small subsets of the Facebook population, including in particular the socialterm·key networks of university students [17–19]. Other studies have analyzed communication patterns and activity amongst segments of the user population [20,21]. Then another thread of research has measured some large-scale networkterm·key properties of the Facebook graphterm·key through sampling, crawling, and other methods to collect networkterm·key data [22,23]. Notably, these methodologies have no way of distinguishing between active and stale accounts. Unlike these studies, we analyze the entire Facebook graphterm·key in anonymized form and focus on the set of active user accounts reliably corresponding to people.

We defined a user of Facebook as an active member of the social networkterm·dom if they logged into the site in the last 28 days from our time of measurement in May 2011 and had at least one Facebook friend. We note that this definition is not precisely Facebook’s ordinary definition of active user, and therefore some of our statistics differ slightly from company statistics. According to our definition of active, the population of active Facebook usersterm·key consisted of around n = 721 million individuals at the time of our measurements. For comparison, estimating that the world’s population was around 6.9 billion people in May 2011 means that the networkterm·key includes roughly 10 percent of the world’s population. Restricting the comparison to individuals age 13 or more and with access to the internet (the set of individuals eligible to have Facebook accounts) would put this percentage significantly higher. There were 68.7 billion friendship edges at the time of our measurements, so the average Facebook user in our study had around 190 Facebook friends. Our analysis predated the existence of ‘subscriptions’ on Facebook, and in this work we study only reciprocal Facebook friendshipsterm·key.

We also analyzed the subgraph of 149 million U.S. Facebook usersterm·key. Using population estimates from the U.S. Census Bereau for 2011, there are roughly 260 million individuals in the U.S. over the age of 13 and therefore eligible to create a Facebook account. Within the U.S., the Facebook social networkterm·dom therefore includes more than half the eligible population. This subpopulation had 15.9 billion edges, so the average U.S. user was friends with around 214 other U.S. usersterm·key. This higher average value may reflect Facebook’s deeper adoption in the U.S. as of May 2011.

Our goals with characterizing Facebook’s social networkterm·dom are two-fold. First, we aim to advance the collective knowledge of socialterm·key networks and satisfy widespread curiosity about socialterm·key relationships as embodied in Facebook. Second, we hope to focus the development of graphterm·key algorithms and networkterm·key analysis tools towards a realistic representation of these relationships. Towards these goals, we provide an accurate description of Facebook’s social networkterm·dom here.

Degree distributionterm·mth. A fundamental quantity measured repeatedly in empirical studies of networks has been the degree distributionterm·mth pk. The degreeterm·key k of an individual is the number of friends that individual has, and pk is the fraction of individuals in the networkterm·key who have exactly k friends. We computed the degree distributionterm·mth of active Facebook usersterm·key across the entire global population and also within the subpopulation of American usersterm·key. The global and U.S. degreeterm·key distributions are shown in Fig. 1, displayed on a log-log scale.

Because the distribution for the U.S. is quite similar to that of the entire population, we focus our attention on the global degree distributionterm·mth. The distribution is nearly monotonically decreasing, except for a small anomaly near 20 friends. This kink is due to forces within the Facebook product to encourage low friend count individuals in particular to gain more friends until they reach 20 friends. The distribution shows a clear cutoff at 5000 friends, a limit imposed by Facebook on the number of friends at the time of our measurements. Note that since 5000 is nowhere near the number of Facebook usersterm·key, each user is clearly friends with a vanishing fraction of the Facebook population. Reflecting most observed socialterm·key networks, our socialterm·key relationships are sparse.

Indeed, most individuals have a moderate number of friends on Facebook, less than 200, while a much smaller population have many hundreds or even thousands of friends. The median friend count for global usersterm·key in our study was 99. Path lengths. When studying a networkterm·key’s structure, the distribution of distances between vertices is a truly macroscopic property of fundamental interest. Here we characterize the neighborhood functions and the average pairwise distances of the Facebook and U.S. networks.

Formally, the neighborhood function N (h) of a graphterm·key describes the number of pairs of vertices (u, v) such that u is reachable from v along a path in the networkterm·key with h edges or less. Given the neighborhood function, the diameter of a graphterm·key is simply the maximum distance between any pair of vertices in the graphterm·key. The diameter is an extremal measure, and it is commonly considered less interesting than the full neighborhood function, which measures what percentile of vertex pairs are within a given distance. The exact diameter can be wildly distorted by the presence of a single ill-connected path in some peripheral region of the graphterm·key, while the neighborhood function and its average are thought to robustly capture the ‘typical’ distances between pairs of vertices.

Like many other graphs, the Facebook graphterm·key does not have paths between all pairs of vertices. This does not prevent us from describing the networkterm·key using the neighborhood function though. As we shall see in the next section, the vast majority of the networkterm·key consists of one large connected componentterm·def and therefore the neighborhood function is representative of the overwhelming majority of pairs of vertices. Friends of Friends. An important property of graphs to consider when designing algorithms is the number of vertices that are within two hops of an initial vertex. This property determines the extent to which graphterm·key traversal algorithms, such as breadth-first search, are feasible. Discussion

In this paper, we have characterized the structure of the Facebook socialterm·key graphterm·key using many metrics and tools. To our knowledge, our study is the largest structural analysis of a social networkterm·dom performed to date.

We began by characterizing the degree distributionterm·mth which was shown to be skewed with a large variance in friendship count. Unlike many other networks, a pure power-law was seen to be inappropriate for the degree distributionterm·mth of Facebook, although hubs certainly exist.

The small-world effectterm·dom and six degrees of separation were then confirmed on a truly global scale. The average distance between vertices of the giant component was found to be 4.7, and we interpret this result as indicating that individuals on Facebook have potentially tremendous reach. Shared content only needs to advance a few steps across Facebook’s social networkterm·dom to reach a substantial fraction of the world’s population.

We have found that the Facebook social networkterm·dom is nearly fully connected, has short average path lengths, and high clusteringterm·key. Many other empirical networks, socialterm·key and non-socialterm·key, have also been observed with these characteristics, and Watts and Strogatz called networks with these properties ‘small- world networks’ [44].

Because our friends have more friends than we do, individuals on Facebook have a surprisingly large number of friends-of-friends. Further, our friends are highly clustered and our friendshipsterm·key possess dense cores, a phenomena not noticed in smaller socialterm·key networks. This neighborhood structure has substantial algorithmic implications for graphterm·key traversal computations. Breadth-first search out to distance two will potentially query a large number of individuals due to positive degreeterm·key correlation, and then will follow many edges to individuals already found due to the clusteringterm·key.

We also performed an exploratory comparison of the networkterm·key structure with user traits including login behavior, age, gender, and country. We found interesting mixing patterns, including that ‘your friends login to Facebook more than you do’ and a strong preference for friends of around the same age and from the same country.

Community structure was shown to be clearly evident in the global networkterm·key, at the scale of friendshipsterm·key between and within countries. Countries in turn were seen to themselves exhibit a modular organization, largely dictated by geographical distance. Unlike prior studies of networks, this community structure was discovered without much effort, indicating the significant structural insights that can be derived from basic demographic information.

While our computations have elucidated many aspects of the structure of the world’s largest social networkterm·dom, we have certainly not exhausted the possibilities of networkterm·key analysis. We hope that this in-

formation is useful both for socialterm·key science research and for the design of the next-generation of graphterm·key algorithms and social networkterm·dom analysis techniques.

Materials and Methods

Unless otherwise noted, calculations were performed on a Hadoop cluster with 2,250 machines, using the Hadoop/Hive data analysis framework developed at Facebook [45, 46].

For analyzing networkterm·key neighborhoodsterm·key, 5,000 usersterm·key were randomly selected using reservoir sampling for each of 100 log-spaced neighborhood sizes, creating a sample of 500,000 usersterm·key for which the analysis was performed. The percentiles shown for the clustering coefficientterm·mth and degeneracy for each neighborhood size are therefore empirical percentiles from this sample population of 5,000 usersterm·key.

To analyze the component structure of the networkterm·key, we used the Newman-Zipf (NZ) algorithm [47]. The NZ algorithm, a type of Union-Find algorithm with path compression, records component structure dynamically as edges are added to a networkterm·key that begins completely empty of edges. When all edges are added, the algorithm has computed the component structure of the networkterm·key. Crucially for our purposes, the NZ algorithm does not require the edges to be retained in memory. We apply it to the Facebook networkterm·key on a single computer with 64GB of RAM by streaming over the list of edges.

For path length calculations, neighborhood functions were computed on a single 24-core machine with 72 GB of RAM using the HyperANF algorithm [48], averaging across 10 runs. For the technical details behind this formidable computation, see [27].
