Abstract

We propose a new system for generating artterm·key. The system generates artterm·key by look- ing at artterm·key and learning about styleterm·dom; and becomes creativeterm·key by increasing the arousalterm·key potential of the generatedterm·key artterm·key by deviatingterm·key from the learned styles. We build over Generative Adversarial Networks (GANterm·key), which have shown the ability to learn to generate novel images simulating a given distribution. This ability is fundamental to show that Artificial Intelligence algorithms are in fact intelligent. In terms of visual artterm·key, several systems have been proposed to

Within the computational creativityterm·mth literature, different algorithms have been proposed focused on investigating various and effective ways of exploring the creativeterm·key space. Several approaches have used an evolutionary process wherein the algorithm iterates by generating candidates, evalu- ating them using a fitness function, and then modifying them to improve the fitness score for the next iteration (e.g. [14, 6]). Recent systems have emphasized the role of perception and cognition in the creativeterm·key process [4, 5, 10].

The goal of this paper is to investigate a computational creativeterm·key system for artterm·key generation with- out involving a human artist in the creativeterm·key process, but nevertheless involving human creativeterm·key products in the learning process. An essential component in artterm·key-generating algorithms is relating their creativeterm·key process to artterm·key that has been produced by human artists throughout time. We believe this is important because a human’s creativeterm·key process utilizes prior experience of and exposure to artterm·key. A human artist is continuously exposed to other artists’ work, and has been exposed to a wide variety of artterm·key for all of his/her life. What remains largely unknown is how human artists integrate their knowledge of past artterm·key with their ability to generate new forms. A theory is needed to model how to integrate exposure to artterm·key with the creation of artterm·key.

Colin Martindale (1943-2008) proposed a psychology-based theory that explains new artterm·key creation[15].

The approach proposed in this paper is inspired by Martindale’s principle of least effort and his explanation of styleterm·dom breaks. Among theories that try to explain progress in artterm·key, we find Martindale’s theory to be computationally feasible.

Deep neural networks have recently played a transformative role in advancing artificial intel- ligence across various application domains. We argue that such networks are limited in their ability to generate creativeterm·key products in their original design. 2 Methodology

2.1 Background

The proposed approach is motivated from the theory suggested by D. E. Berlyne (1924-1976). Berlyne argued that the psychophysical concept of “arousalterm·key” has a great relevance for studying aesthetic phenomena [3]. “Level of arousalterm·key” measures how alert or excited a human being is. The

level of arousalterm·key varies from the lowest level, when a person is asleep or relaxed, to the highest level when s/he is violent, in a fury, or in a passionate situation [2]. Among different mecha- nisms of arousalterm·key, of particular importance and relevance to artterm·key are properties of external stimulus patterns [3].

The term “arousalterm·key potential” refers to the properties of stimulus patterns that lead to raising arousalterm·key. Besides other psychophysical and ecological properties of stimulus patterns, Berlyne emphasized that the most significant arousalterm·key-raising properties for aesthetics are noveltyterm·key, surpris- ingness, complexity, ambiguity, and puzzlingness. He coined the term collative variables to refer to these properties collectively.

Noveltyterm·key refers to the degree a stimulus differs from what an observer has seen/experienced be- fore. Surprisingness refers to the degree a stimulus disagrees with expectation. Surprisingness is not necessarily correlated with noveltyterm·key, for example it can stem from lack of noveltyterm·key. Unlike noveltyterm·key and surprisingness which rely on inter-stimulus comparisons of similarity and differences, complexity is an intra-stimulus property that increases as the number of independent elements in a stimulus grows. Ambiguity refers to the conflict between the semantic and syntactic informa- tion in a stimulus. Puzzlingness refers to the ambiguity due to multiple, potentially inconsistent, meanings.

Several studies have shown that people prefer stimulus with a moderate arousalterm·key potential [2, 20]. Too little arousalterm·key potential is considered boring, and too much activates the aversion system, which results in negative response. This behavior is explained by the Wundt curveterm·def that correlates the arousalterm·key potential with the hedonic response [3, 24]. Berlyne also studied arousalterm·key moderating mechanisms. Of particular importance in artterm·key is habituation, which refers to decreased arousalterm·key in response to repetitions of a stimulus [3].

Martindale emphasized the importance of habituation in deriving the artterm·key-producing system [15]. If artists keep producing similar works of arts, this directly reduces the arousalterm·key potential and hence the desirability of that artterm·key. Therefore, at any point of time, the artterm·key-producing system will try to increase the arousalterm·key potential of produced artterm·key. In other words, habituation forms a constant pres- sure to change artterm·key. However, this increase has to be within the minimum amount necessary to compensate for habituation without falling into the negative hedonic range, according to Wundt curveterm·def findings (“stimuli that are slightly rather than vastly supernormal are preferred”). Martindale called this the principle of “least effort”. Therefore, there is an opposite pressure that leads to a graduated pace of change in artterm·key.

2.2 Artterm·key Generating Agent

We propose a model for an artterm·key-generating agent, and then propose a functioning model using a variant of GANterm·key to make it creativeterm·key. The agent’s goal is to generate artterm·key with increased levels of arousalterm·key potential in a constrained way without activating the aversion system and falling into the negative hedonic range. In other words, the agent tries to generate artterm·key that is novel, but not too novel. This criterion is common in many computationally creativeterm·key systems, however it is not easy to find a way to achieve that goal given the infinite possibilities in the creativeterm·key space.

In our model the artterm·key-generating agent has a memory that encodes the artterm·key it has been exposed to, and can be continuously updated with the addition of new artterm·key. The agent uses this encoded memory in an indirect way while generating new artterm·key with a restrained increase in arousalterm·key potential. While there are several ways to increase the arousalterm·key potential, in this paper we focus on building

an agent that tries to increase the stylistic ambiguity and deviations from styleterm·dom norms, while at the same time, avoiding moving too far away from what is accepted as artterm·key. The agent tries to explore the creativeterm·key space by deviatingterm·key from the established styleterm·dom norms and thereby generates new artterm·key.

There are two types of ambiguities that are expected in the generatedterm·key artterm·key by the proposed net- work; one is by design and the other one is inherent. Almost all computer-generatedterm·key artterm·key might be ambiguous because the artterm·key generatedterm·key typically does not have clear figures or an interpretable subject matter. Because of this, Heath et al argued that the creativeterm·key machine would need to have perceptual ability (be able to see) in order to be able to generate plausible creativeterm·key artterm·key [10]. This limited perceptual ability is what causes the inherent ambiguity. Typically, this type of ambiguity results in users being able to tell right away that the work is generatedterm·key by a machine rather than a human artist. Even though several styles of artterm·key developed in the 20th century might lack rec- ognizable figures or lucid subject matter, human observers usually are not fooled into confusing computer-generatedterm·key artterm·key with human-generatedterm·key artterm·key.

Because of this inherent ambiguity people always think of computer-generatedterm·key artterm·key as being hallucination-like. The Guardian commented on a the images generatedterm·key by Google DeepDream [16] by “Most, however, look like dorm-room mandalas, or the kind of digital psychedelia you might expect to find on the cover of a Terrence McKenna book”1. Others commented on it as being “daz- zling, druggy, and creepy” 2. This negative reaction might be explained as a result of too much arousalterm·key, which results in negative hedonics according to the Wundt curveterm·def.

The other type of ambiguity in the artterm·key generatedterm·key by the proposed agent is stylistic ambiguity, which is intentional by design. The rational is that creativeterm·key artists would eventually break from established styles and explore new ways of expression to increase the arousalterm·key potential of their artterm·key, as Martindale suggested. As suggested by DiPaola and Gabora, “creators often work within a very structured domain, following rules that they eventually break free of” [6].

The proposed artterm·key-generating agent is realized by a model called Creative Adversarial Networkterm·ai (CAN), which we will describe next. The network is designed to generate artterm·key that does not follow established artterm·key movements or styles, but instead tries to generate artterm·key that maximally confuses human viewers as to which styleterm·dom it belongs to.

2.3 GANterm·key: Emulative and not Creativeterm·key

Generative Adversarial Network (GANterm·key) has two sub networks, a generator and a discriminatorterm·key. The discriminatorterm·key has access to a set of images (training images). The discriminatorterm·key tries to dis- criminate between “real” images (from the training set) and “fake” images generatedterm·key by the gener- ator. The generator tries to generate images similar to the training set without seeing these images. The generator starts by generating random images and receives a signal from the discriminatorterm·key whether the discriminatorterm·key finds them real or fake. At equilibrium the discriminatorterm·key should not be able to tell the difference between the images generatedterm·key by the generator and the actual images in the training set, hence the generator succeeds in generating images that come from the same distribution as the training set.

Let us now assume that we trained a GANterm·key model on images of paintings. Since the generator is trained to generate images that fool the discriminatorterm·key to believe it is coming from the training

In that case the discriminatorterm·key will right away be fooled into believing that the generator is generating artterm·key, while in fact it is already existing artterm·key, and hence not novel and not creativeterm·key.

There have been extensions to GANs that facilitate generating images conditioned on cate- gories (e.g., [18]) or captions (e.g., [19]). We can think of a GANterm·key that can be designed and trained to generate images of different artterm·key styles or different artterm·key genres by providing such labels with train- ing. This might be able to generate artterm·key that looks like, for example, Renaissance, Impressionism, or Cubism. However that does not lead to anything creativeterm·key either. This highlights the fundamental limitation of using GANs in generating creativeterm·key works.

2.4 From being Emulative to being Creativeterm·key

In the proposed Creative Adversarial Networkterm·ai (CAN), the generator is designed to receive two signals from the discriminatorterm·key that act as two contradictory forces to achieve three points: 1) generate novel works, 2) the novel work should not too novel, i.e., it should not be too far away from the distribution or it will generate too much arousalterm·key, thereby activating the aversion system and falling into the negative hedonic range according to the Wundt curveterm·def, 3) the generatedterm·key work should increase the stylistic ambiguity.

Similar to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANterm·key), the proposed network has two adversary networks, a discriminatorterm·key and a generator. The discriminatorterm·key has access to a large set of artterm·key asso- ciated with styleterm·dom labels (Renaissance, Baroque, Impressionism, Expressionism, etc.) and uses it to learn to discriminate between styles. The generator does not have access to any artterm·key. It generates artterm·key starting from a random input, but unlike GANterm·key, it receives two signals from the discriminatorterm·key for any work it generates. The first signal is the discriminatorterm·key’s classification of “artterm·key or not artterm·key”. In traditional GANterm·key, this signal enables the generator to change its weights to generate images that more frequently will deceive the discriminatorterm·key as to whether it is coming from the same distribu- tion. Since the discriminatorterm·key in our case is trained on artterm·key, this will signal whether the discriminatorterm·key thinks the generatedterm·key artterm·key is coming from the same distribution as the actual artterm·key it knows about. In that sense, this signal flags whether the discriminatorterm·key thinks the image presented to it is “artterm·key or not artterm·key”. Since the generator only receives this signal, it will eventually converge to generate images that will emulate artterm·key.

The second signal the generator receives is a signal about how well the discriminatorterm·key can clas- sify the generatedterm·key artterm·key into established styles. If the generator generates images that the discrimina- tor thinks are artterm·key and also can easily classify into one of the established styles, then the generator would have fooled the discriminatorterm·key into believing it generatedterm·key actual artterm·key that fits within established styles. In contrast, the creativeterm·key generator will try to generate artterm·key that confuses the discriminatorterm·key. On one hand it tries to fool the discriminatorterm·key to think it is “artterm·key,” and on the other hand it tries to confuse the discriminatorterm·key about the styleterm·dom of the work generatedterm·key.

We modified the GANterm·key loss function to achieve the vision explained in the previous section. Fig- ure 2 illustrates the architecture. We added a styleterm·dom classification loss and a styleterm·dom ambiguity loss. Maximizing the stylistic ambiguity can be achieved by maximizing the styleterm·dom class posterior en- tropy. Hence, we need to design the loss such that the generator G produces an image x ∼ pdata and, meanwhile, maximizes the entropy of p(c|x) (i.e. styleterm·dom class posterior) for the generatedterm·key im- ages. However, instead of maximizing the class posterior entropy, we minimize the cross entropy between the class posterior and a uniform target distribution. Similar to entropy that is maximized when the class posteriors (i.e., p(c|G(z))) are equiprobable, cross entropy with uniform target dis- tribution will be minimized when the classes are equiprobable. So both objectives will be optimal when the classes are equiprobable. However, the difference is that the cross entropy will go up sharply at the boundary since it goes to infinity if any class posterior approaches 1 (or zero), while entropy goes to zero at this boundary condition (see Figure 3). Therefore, using the cross entropy results in a hefty penalty if the generatedterm·key image is classified to one of the classes with high proba- bility. 4.2 Qualitative Validation

Assessing the creativity of artifacts generatedterm·key by the machine is an open and hard question. As noted by Colton 2008, aesthetic assessment of an artifact is different from the creativity assess- ment [4]. Figures 1, Figures 4.2 and 7 show samples of the generatedterm·key images by the proposed CAN model. The images generatedterm·key by CAN do not look likes traditional artterm·key, in terms of standard genres (portrait, landscapes, religious paintings, still life, etc.). We also do not see any recogniz- able figures. Many of the images seems abstract. Is that simply because it fails to emulate the artterm·key distribution, or is it because it tries to generate novel images? Is it at all creativeterm·key? These are hard questions which we will try to get an insight to help answering them.

We evaluated the images generatedterm·key by three baseline models in comparison to the ones gener- ated by the proposed CAN model to gain an insight into the differences qualitatively and quantita- tively. All the models were trained on the same artterm·key dataset, as shown in table 1.

The first two baselines are variants of the original DCGAN [18] model trained on artterm·key data. Since GANterm·key aims to emulate the training distribution, we should expect training the model on artterm·key data would result in generating images that show recognizable figures, subject matters, artterm·key genres, and styles. The first baseline model is the original DCGAN [18] model trained on artterm·key data. This model generates images with 64x64 resolution. Although trained on artterm·key data, this model failed to generate images that emulate the trained artterm·key. The generatedterm·key samples did not show any recognizable figures or artterm·key genres or styles. The top panel of Figure 4 shows samples of the generatedterm·key images; here we show the six top and six bottom ranked images based on human subject evaluation in Experiment I below.

The second baseline model is the original DCGAN [18] model after adding two more layers to the generator to increase the resolution to 256x256, i.e., the generator here has the same exact architecture as the CAN model. We also trained this model on the artterm·key collection. The generatedterm·key samples show significant improvement; we can clearly see aesthetically appealing compositional structures and color contrasts in the resulting images. However, the generatedterm·key images also did not show any recognizable figures, subject matters or artterm·key genres. The middle panel of Figure 4 shows samples of the generatedterm·key images; here we show the six top and six bottom ranked images based on human subject evaluation in Experiment II below.

The third model is a variant of the proposed CAN model with the styleterm·dom classification loss (without the styleterm·dom ambiguity loss). In that model the discriminatorterm·key learns to discriminate between styleterm·dom classes along learning the artterm·key distribution. The generator has exactly the same loss as the GANterm·key model, i.e., only try to deceive the discriminatorterm·key by emulating the training distribution. In other words, unlike the two first baselines that learn about artterm·key (artterm·key/nor artterm·key), this model also learn about styles classes in artterm·key. We refer to this model by styleterm·dom-classification-CAN. The generatedterm·key images of that model show significant improvement in actually emulating the artterm·key distribution, in the sense that we can see lots of hallucination of portraits, landscapes, architectures, religious subject matter, etc. We didn’t see any of that on the first two baselines. The bottom panel of Figure 4 shows samples of the generatedterm·key images, which show portraits, landscapes, architecture elements, religious themes, etc. This baseline shows that the CAN model, without the styleterm·dom ambiguity, can better emulate the artterm·key distribution by learning about styleterm·dom classes, however not creativeterm·key (Experiment IV below is designed to quantitatively validate this claim).
