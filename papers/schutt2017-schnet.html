Abstract

Deep learningterm·ai has the potential to revolutionize quantum chemistryterm·dom as it is ideally suited to learn representations for structured data and speed up the exploration of chemicalterm·key space. While convolutionalterm·key neural networks have proven to be the first choice for images, audio and video data, the atomsterm·key in moleculesterm·key are not restricted to a grid. Instead, their precise locations contain essential physical information, that would get lost if discretized. Thus, we propose to use continuous- filter convolutionalterm·key layers to be able to model local correlations without requiring the data to lie on a grid. We apply those layers in SchNet: a novel deep learningterm·ai architecture modeling quantumterm·key interactions in moleculesterm·key. We obtain a joint model for the total energyterm·key and interatomicterm·key forcesterm·key that follows fundamental quantumterm·key- chemicalterm·key principles. Our architecture achieves state-of-the-art performance for benchmarks of equilibrium moleculesterm·key and molecular dynamics trajectories. Finally, we introduce a more challenging benchmark with chemicalterm·key and structural variations that suggests the path for further work.

Introduction

The discovery of novel moleculesterm·key and materials with desired properties is crucial for applications such as batteries, catalysis and drug design. However, the vastness of chemicalterm·key compound space and the computational cost of accurate quantumterm·key-chemicalterm·key calculations prevent an exhaustive explo- ration. In recent years, there have been increased efforts to use machine learning for the accelerated discovery of moleculesterm·key and materials with desired properties [1–9]. However, these methods are only applied to stable systems in so-called equilibrium, i.e., local minima of the potential energy surfaceterm·def E(r1, . . . , rn) where ri is the position of atom i. Data sets such as the established QM9 benchmark [10] contain only equilibrium moleculesterm·key. Predicting stable atom arrangements is in itself an important challenge in quantum chemistryterm·dom and material science.

In general, it is not clear how to obtain equilibrium conformations without optimizing the atom positions. Therefore, we need to compute both the total energyterm·key E(r1, . . . , rn) and the forcesterm·key acting on the atomsterm·key

One possibility is to use a less computationally costly, however, also less accurate quantumterm·key-chemicalterm·key approximation. Instead, we choose to extend the domain of our machine learning model to both compositional (chemicalterm·key) and configurational (structural) degrees of freedom.

In this work, we aim to learn a representation for moleculesterm·key using equilibrium and non-equilibrium conformations. Such a general representation for atomistic systems should follow fundamental quantumterm·key-mechanical principles. Most importantly, the predicted force field has to be curl-free. Otherwise, it would be possible to follow a circular trajectory of atom positions such that the energyterm·key keeps increasing, i.e., breaking the law of energyterm·key conservation. Furthermore, the potential energy surfaceterm·def as well as its partial derivatives have to be smooth, e.g., in order to be able to perform geometry optimization. Beyond that, it is beneficial that the model incorporates the invariance of the molecular energyterm·key with respect to rotation, translation and atom indexing. Being able to model both chemicalterm·key and conformational variations constitutes an important step towards ML-driven quantumterm·key-chemicalterm·key exploration.

This work provides the following key contributions:

• We propose continuous-filterterm·key convolutionalterm·key (cfconv) layers as a means to move beyond grid-bound data such as images or audio towards modeling objects with arbitrary positions such as astronomical observations or atomsterm·key in moleculesterm·key and materials.

• We propose SchNet: a neural network specifically designed to respect essential quantumterm·key- chemicalterm·key constraints. In particular, we use the proposed cfconv layers in R3 to model interactions of atomsterm·key at arbitrary positions in the molecule. SchNet delivers both rotationally invariant energyterm·key prediction and rotationally equivariant force predictions. We obtain a smooth potential energy surfaceterm·def and the resulting force-field is guaranteed to be energyterm·key- conserving.

• We present a new, challenging benchmark – ISO17 – including both chemicalterm·key and confor- mational changes3. We show that training with forcesterm·key improves generalization in this setting as well.

2 Related work

Previous work has used neural networks and Gaussian processes applied to hand-crafted features to fit potential energyterm·key surfaces [11–16]. Graph convolutionalterm·key networks for circular fingerprint [17] and molecular graph convolutions [18] learn representations for moleculesterm·key of arbitrary size. They encode the molecular structure using neighborhood relationships as well as bond features, e.g., one-hot encodings of single, double and triple bonds. In the following, we briefly review the related work that will be used in our empirical evaluation: gradient domain machine learning (GDML), deep tensor neural networks (DTNN) and enn-s2s.

Gradient-domain machine learning (GDML) ChmielapersName et al. [19] proposed GDML as a method to construct force fields that explicitly obey the law of energyterm·key conservation. GDML captures the relationship between energyterm·key and interatomicterm·key forcesterm·key (see Eq. 1) by training the gradient of the energyterm·key estimator. The functional relationship between atomic coordinates and interatomicterm·key forcesterm·key is thus learned directly and energyterm·key predictions are obtained by re-integration. However, GDML does not scale well due to its kernel matrix growing quadratically with the number of atomsterm·key as well as the number of examples. Beyond that, it is not designed to represent different compositions of atom types unlike SchNet, DTNN and enn-s2s.

Deep tensor neural networks (DTNN) SchüttpersName et al. [20] proposed the DTNN for moleculesterm·key that are inspired by the many-body Hamiltonian applied to the interactions of atomsterm·key. They have been shown to reach chemicalterm·key accuracy on a small set of molecular dynamics trajectories as well as QM9. Even though the DTNN shares the invariances with our proposed architecture, its interaction layers lack the continuous-filterterm·key convolution interpretation. It falls behind in accuracy compared to SchNet and enn-s2s.

enn-s2s Gilmer et al. [21] proposed the enn-s2s as a variant of message-passing neural networks that uses bond type features in addition to interatomicterm·key distances. It achieves state-of-the-art performance on all properties of the QM9 benchmark [21]. Unfortunately, it cannot be used for molecular dynamics predictions (MD-17). 3 Continuous-filterterm·key convolutions

In deep learningterm·ai, convolutionalterm·key layers operate on discretized signals such as image pixels [22, 23], video frames [24] or digital audio data [25]. While it is sufficient to define the filter on the same grid in these cases, this is not possible for unevenly spaced inputs such as the atom positions of a molecule (see Fig. 1). Other examples include astronomical observations [26], climate data [27] and the financial market [28]. Commonly, this can be solved by a re-sampling approach defining a representation on a grid [7, 29, 30]. However, choosing an appropriate interpolation scheme is a challenge on its own and, possibly, requires a large number of grid points. Therefore, various extensions of convolutionalterm·key layers even beyond the Euclidean space exist, e.g., for graphs [31, 32] and 3d shapes[33]. 4 SchNet

SchNet is designed to learn a representation for the prediction of molecular energies and atomic forcesterm·key. It reflects fundamental physical laws including invariance to atom indexing and translation, a smooth energyterm·key prediction w.r.t. atom positions as well as energyterm·key-conservation of the predicted force fields. The energyterm·key and force predictions are rotationally invariant and equivariant, respectively.

As SchNet yields rotationally invariant energyterm·key predictions, the force predictions are rotationally equivariant by construction. The model has to be at least twice differentiable to allow for gradient descent of the force loss. We chose a shifted softplus ssp(x) = ln(0.5ex + 0.5) as non-linearity throughout the network in order to obtain a smooth potential energy surfaceterm·def. 5 Experiments and results

In this section, we apply the SchNet to three different quantum chemistryterm·dom datasets: QM9, MD17 and ISO17. We designed the experiments such that each adds another aspect towards modeling chemicalterm·key space. While QM9 only contains equilibrium moleculesterm·key, for MD17 we predict conformational changes of molecular dynamics of single moleculesterm·key. Finally, we present ISO17 combining both chemicalterm·key and structural changes.

For all datasets, we report mean absolute errors in kcal/mol for the energies and in kcal/mol/Å for the forcesterm·key. The architecture of the network was fixed after an evaluation on the MD17 data sets for benzene and ethanol (see supplement). In each experiment, we split the data into a training set of given size N and use a validation set of 1,000 examples for early stopping. The remaining data is used as test set. All models are trained with SGD using the ADAM optimizer [39] with 32 moleculesterm·key per mini-batch. We use an initial learning rate of 10−3 and an exponential learning rate decay with ratio 0.96 every 100,000 steps. The model used for testing is obtained using an exponential moving average over weights with decay rate 0.99.

5.2 MD17 – conformational degrees of freedom

MD17 is a collection of eight molecular dynamics simulations for small organic moleculesterm·key. These data sets were introduced by ChmielapersName et al. [19] for prediction of energyterm·key-conserving force fields using GDML. Each of these consists of a trajectory of a single molecule covering a large variety of conformations. Here, the task is to predict energies and forcesterm·key using a separate model for each trajectory. This molecule-wise training is motivated by the need for highly-accurate force predictions when doing molecular dynamics.

Table 2 shows the performance of SchNet using 1,000 and 50,000 training examples in comparison with GDML and DTNN. Using the smaller data set, GDML achieves remarkably accurate energyterm·key and force predictions despite being only trained on forcesterm·key. The energies are only used to fit the integration constant. As mentioned before, GDML does not scale well with the number of atomsterm·key and training examples. Therefore, it cannot be trained on 50,000 examples. The DTNN was evaluated only on four of these MD trajectories using the larger training set [20]. Note that the enn-s2s cannot be used on this dataset due to discontinuities in its inferred potential energy surfaceterm·def.

We trained SchNet using just energies and using both energies and forcesterm·key. While the energyterm·key-only model shows high errors for the small training set, the model including forcesterm·key achieves energyterm·key predictions comparable to GDML. In particular, we observe that SchNet outperforms GDML on the more flexible moleculesterm·key malonaldehyde and ethanol, while GDML reaches much lower force errors on the remaining MD trajectories that all include aromatic rings.

The real strength of SchNet is its scalability, as it outperforms the DTNN in three of four data sets using 50,000 training examples using only energies in training. Including force information, SchNet consistently obtains accurate energies and forcesterm·key with errors below 0.12 kcal/mol and 0.33 kcal/mol/Å, respectively. Remarkably, when training on energies and forcesterm·key using 1,000 training examples, SchNet performs better than training the same model on energies alone for 50,000 examples.

ISO17 – chemicalterm·key and conformational degrees of freedom

As the next step towards quantumterm·key-chemicalterm·key exploration, we demonstrate the capability of SchNet to represent a complex potential energy surfaceterm·def including conformational and chemicalterm·key changes. We present a new dataset – ISO17 – where we consider short MD trajectories of 129 isomers, i.e., chemically different moleculesterm·key with the same number and types of atomsterm·key. In contrast to MD17, we train a joint model across different moleculesterm·key. We calculate energies and interatomicterm·key forcesterm·key from short MD trajectories of 129 moleculesterm·key drawn randomly from the largest set of isomers in QM9. While the composition of all included moleculesterm·key is C7O2H10, the chemicalterm·key structures are fundamentally different. With each trajectory consisting of 5,000 conformations, the data set consists of 645,000 labeled examples.

We consider two scenarios with this dataset: In the first variant, the molecular graph structures present in training are also present in the test data. This demonstrates how well our model is able to represent a complex potential energy surfaceterm·def with chemicalterm·key and conformational changes. In the more challenging scenario, the test data contains a different subset of moleculesterm·key. Here we evaluate the generalization of our model to previously unseen chemicalterm·key structures. We predict forcesterm·key and energies in both cases and compare to the mean predictor as a baseline. We draw a subset of 4,000 steps from 80% of the MD trajectories for training and validation. This leaves us with a separate test set for each scenario: (1) the unseen 1,000 conformations of molecule trajectories included in the training set and (2) all 5,000 conformations of the remaining 20% of moleculesterm·key not included in training.

Table 3 shows the performance of the SchNet on both test sets. Our proposed model reaches chemicalterm·key accuracy for the prediction of energies and forcesterm·key for the test set of known moleculesterm·key. Including forcesterm·key in the training improves the performance here as well as on the set of unseen moleculesterm·key. This shows that using force information does not only help to accurately predict nearby conformations of a single molecule, but indeed helps to generalize across chemicalterm·key compound space.

6 Conclusions
