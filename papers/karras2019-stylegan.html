Abstract

We propose an alternative generatorterm·key architecture for generative adversarial networks, borrowing from style transferterm·dom literature. The new architecture leads to an au- tomatically learned, unsupervised separation of high-level attributes (e.g., pose and identity when trained on human faces) and stochastic variation in the generated imagesterm·key (e.g., freckles, hair), and it enables intuitive, scale-specific control of the synthesisterm·key. The new generatorterm·key improves the state-of-the-art in terms of traditional distribution quality metrics, leads to demonstrably better interpolationterm·key proper- ties, and also better disentangles the latentterm·key factors of varia- tion. To quantify interpolationterm·key quality and disentanglement, we propose two new, automated methods that are applica- ble to any generatorterm·key architecture. Finally, we introduce a new, highly varied and high-quality dataset of human faces.

1. Introduction

The resolution and quality of imagesterm·key produced by gen- erative methods — especially generative adversarial net- works (GAN) [22] — have seen rapid improvement recently [30, 45, 5]. Yet the generators continue to operate as black boxes, and despite recent efforts [3], the understanding of various aspects of the image synthesisterm·dom process, e.g., the ori- gin of stochastic features, is still lacking. The properties of the latentterm·key space are also poorly understood, and the com- monly demonstrated latentterm·key space interpolations [13, 52, 37] provide no quantitative way to compare different generators against each other.

Motivated by style transferterm·dom literature [27], we re-design the generatorterm·key architecture in a way that exposes novel ways to control the image synthesisterm·dom process. Our generatorterm·key starts from a learned constant input and adjusts the “styleterm·key” of the image at each convolution layer based on the latentterm·key code, therefore directly controlling the strength of image features at different scales. Combined with noise injected directly into the networkterm·key, this architectural change leads to automatic, unsupervised separation of high-level attributes

(e.g., pose, identity) from stochastic variation (e.g., freck- les, hair) in the generated imagesterm·key, and enables intuitive scale-specific mixing and interpolationterm·key operations. We do not modify the discriminator or the loss function in any way, and our work is thus orthogonal to the ongoing discus- sion about GAN loss functions, regularization, and hyper- parameters [24, 45, 5, 40, 44, 36].

Our generatorterm·key embeds the input latentterm·key code into an inter- mediate latentterm·key space, which has a profound effect on how the factors of variation are represented in the networkterm·key. The input latentterm·key space must follow the probability density of the training data, and we argue that this leads to some degree of unavoidable entanglement. Our intermediate latentterm·key space is free from that restriction and is therefore allowed to be disentangled. As previous methods for estimating the de- gree of latentterm·key space disentanglement are not directly appli- cable in our case, we propose two new automated metrics — perceptual path length and linear separability — for quanti- fying these aspects of the generatorterm·key. Using these metrics, we show that compared to a traditional generatorterm·key architecture, our generatorterm·key admits a more linear, less entangled represen- tation of different factors of variation.

Finally, we present a new dataset of human faces (Flickr-Faces-HQ, FFHQ) that offers much higher qual- ity and covers considerably wider variation than existing high-resolution datasets (Appendix A). We have made this dataset publicly available, along with our source code and pre-trained networks.1 The accompanying video can be found under the same link.

Much of the work on GAN architectures has focused on improving the discriminator by, e.g., using multiple discriminators [18, 47, 11], multiresolution discrimination [60, 55], or self-attention [63]. 3. Properties of the styleterm·key-based generatorterm·key

Our generatorterm·key architecture makes it possible to control the image synthesisterm·dom via scale-specific modifications to the styles. We can view the mapping networkterm·key and affine trans- formations as a way to draw samples for each styleterm·key from a learned distribution, and the synthesisterm·key networkterm·key as a way to generate a novel image based on a collection of styles. The effects of each styleterm·key are localized in the networkterm·key, i.e., modi- fying a specific subset of the styles can be expected to affect only certain aspects of the image.

To see the reason for this localization, let us consider how the AdaINterm·key operation (Eq. 1) first normalizes each chan- nel to zero mean and unit variance, and only then applies scales and biases based on the styleterm·key. The new per-channel statistics, as dictated by the styleterm·key, modify the relative impor- tance of features for the subsequent convolution operation, but they do not depend on the original statistics because of the normalization. Thus each styleterm·key controls only one convo- lution before being overridden by the next AdaINterm·key operation.

3.1. Styleterm·key mixing

To further encourage the styles to localize, we employ mixing regularization, where a given percentage of imagesterm·key are generated using two random latentterm·key codes instead of one during training. When generating such an image, we sim- ply switch from one latentterm·key code to another — an operation we refer to as styleterm·key mixing — at a randomly selected point in the synthesisterm·key networkterm·key. To be specific, we run two latentterm·key codes z1, z2 through the mapping networkterm·key, and have the corresponding w1, w2 control the styles so that w1 applies before the crossover point and w2 after it. This regular- ization technique prevents the networkterm·key from assuming that adjacent styles are correlated.

3.3. Separation of global effects from stochasticity

The previous sections as well as the accompanying video demonstrate that while changes to the styleterm·key have global ef- fects (changing pose, identity, etc.), the noise affects only inconsequential stochastic variation (differently combed hair, beard, etc.). This observation is in line with styleterm·key trans- fer literature, where it has been established that spatially invariant statistics (Gram matrix, channel-wise mean, vari- ance, etc.) reliably encode the styleterm·key of an image [20, 39] while spatially varying features encode a specific instance. In our styleterm·key-based generatorterm·key, the styleterm·key affects the entire image because complete feature maps are scaled and bi- ased with the same values. Therefore, global effects such as pose, lighting, or background styleterm·key can be controlled co- herently. Meanwhile, the noise is added independently to each pixel and is thus ideally suited for controlling stochas- tic variation. If the networkterm·key tried to control, e.g., pose using the noise, that would lead to spatially inconsistent decisions that would then be penalized by the discriminator. Thus the networkterm·key learns to use the global and local channels appro- priately, without explicit guidance.

4. Disentanglement studies

There are various definitions for disentanglement [54, 50, 2, 7, 19], but a common goal is a latentterm·key space that con- sists of linear subspaces, each of which controls one fac- tor of variation. However, the sampling probability of each combination of factors in Z needs to match the correspond- ing density in the training data. As illustrated in Figure 6, this precludes the factors from being fully disentangled with typical datasets and input latentterm·key distributions.2

A major benefit of our generatorterm·key architecture is that the intermediate latentterm·key space W does not have to support sam-

pling according to any fixed distribution; its sampling den- sity is induced by the learned piecewise continuous map- ping f (z). This mapping can be adapted to “unwarp” W so that the factors of variation become more linear. We posit that there is pressure for the generatorterm·key to do so, as it should be easier to generate realistic imagesterm·key based on a disentan- gled representation than based on an entangled representa- tion. As such, we expect the training to yield a less entan- gled W in an unsupervised setting, i.e., when the factors of variation are not known in advance [10, 35, 49, 8, 26, 32, 7]. Unfortunately the metrics recently proposed for quanti- fying disentanglement [26, 32, 7, 19] require an encoder networkterm·key that maps input imagesterm·key to latentterm·key codes. These met- rics are ill-suited for our purposes since our baseline GAN lacks such an encoder. While it is possible to add an extra networkterm·key for this purpose [8, 12, 15], we want to avoid in- vesting effort into a component that is not a part of the actual solution. To this end, we describe two new ways of quanti- fying disentanglement, neither of which requires an encoder or known factors of variation, and are therefore computable for any image dataset and generatorterm·key.

4.1. Perceptual path length

As noted by LainepersName [37], interpolationterm·key of latentterm·key-space vec- tors may yield surprisingly non-linear changes in the image. For example, features that are absent in either endpoint may appear in the middle of a linear interpolationterm·key path. This is a sign that the latentterm·key space is entangled and the factors of variation are not properly separated. To quantify this ef- fect, we can measure how drastic changes the image under- goes as we perform interpolationterm·key in the latentterm·key space. Intu- itively, a less curved latentterm·key space should result in perceptu- ally smoother transition than a highly curved latentterm·key space.

As a basis for our metric, we use a perceptually-based pairwise image distance [65] that is calculated as a weighted difference between two VGG16 [58] embeddings, where the weights are fit so that the metric agrees with human per- ceptual similarity judgments. If we subdivide a latentterm·key space interpolationterm·key path into linear segments, we can define the total perceptual length of this segmented path as the sum of perceptual differences over each segment, as reported by the image distance metric. A natural definition for the per- ceptual path length would be the limit of this sum under infinitely fine subdivision, but in practice we approximate it using a small subdivision epsilon (cid:15) = 10−4. 4.2. Linear separability

If a latentterm·key space is sufficiently disentangled, it should be possible to find direction vectors that consistently corre- spond to individual factors of variation. We propose another metric that quantifies this effect by measuring how well the latentterm·key-space points can be separated into two distinct sets via a linear hyperplane, so that each set corresponds to a specific binary attribute of the image.

In order to label the generated imagesterm·key, we train auxiliary classification networks for a number of binary attributes, e.g., to distinguish male and female faces. In our tests, the classifiers had the same architecture as the discrimina- tor we use (i.e., same as in [30]), and were trained using the CELEBA-HQ dataset that retains the 40 attributes available in the original CelebA dataset. To measure the separability of one attribute, we generate 200,000 imagesterm·key with z ∼ P (z) and classify them using the auxiliary classification networkterm·key. We then sort the samples according to classifier confidence and remove the least confident half, yielding 100,000 la- beled latentterm·key-space vectors.

For each attribute, we fit a linear SVM to predict the label based on the latentterm·key-space point — z for traditional and w for styleterm·key-based — and classify the points by this plane. We then compute the conditional entropy H(Y |X) where X are the classes predicted by the SVM and Y are the classes deter- mined by the pre-trained classifier. This tells how much ad- ditional information is required to determine the true class of a sample, given that we know on which side of the hy- perplane it lies. A low value suggests consistent latentterm·key space directions for the corresponding factor(s) of variation. score

5. Conclusion

Based on both our results and parallel work by Chen et al. [6], it is becoming clear that the traditional GAN gen- erator architecture is in every way inferior to a styleterm·key-based design. This is true in terms of established quality metrics, and we further believe that our investigations to the separa- tion of high-level attributes and stochastic effects, as well as the linearity of the intermediate latentterm·key space will prove fruitful in improving the understanding and controllability of GAN synthesisterm·key.

We note that our average path length metric could easily be used as a regularizer during training, and perhaps some variant of the linear separability metric could act as one, too. In general, we expect that methods for directly shaping the intermediate latentterm·key space during training will provide interesting avenues for future work.

B. Truncation trick in W

If we consider the distribution of training data, it is clear that areas of low density are poorly represented and thus likely to be difficult for the generatorterm·key to learn. This is a significant open problem in all generative modeling tech- niques. However, it is known that drawing latentterm·key vectors from a truncated [42, 5] or otherwise shrunk [34] sampling space tends to improve average image quality, although some amount of variation is lost.

We can follow a similar strategy. To begin, we compute the center of mass of W as ̄w = Ez∼P (z)[f (z)]. In case of FFHQ this point represents a sort of an average face (Fig- ure 8, ψ = 0). We can then scale the deviation of a given w from the center as w(cid:48) = ̄w + ψ(w − ̄w), where ψ < 1. While Brock et al. [5] observe that only a subset of net- works is amenable to such truncation even when orthogonal regularization is used, truncation in W space seems to work reliably even without changes to the loss function.

We build upon the official TensorFlow [1] implemen- tation of Progressive GANs by KarraspersName et al. For our improved baseline (B in Table 1), we make sev- eral modifications to improve the overall result quality. We replace the nearest-neighbor up/downsampling in both net- works with bilinear sampling, which we implement by low- pass filtering the activations with a separable 2nd order bi- nomial filter after each upsampling layer and before each downsampling layer [64]. For our styleterm·key-based generatorterm·key (F in Table 1), we use leaky ReLU [41] with α = 0.2 and equalized learning rate [30] for all layers. We use the same feature map counts in our convolution layers as KarraspersName et al. [30]. We initialize all weights of the convolutional, fully-connected, and affine transform layers using N (0, 1). The constant input in synthesisterm·key networkterm·key is initialized to one.
