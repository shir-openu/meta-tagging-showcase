Abstract

We propose a criterion for discriminationterm·dom against a specified sensitive attributeterm·key in su- pervised learning, where the goal is to predict some target based on available features. Assuming data about the predictorterm·key, target, and membership in the protected group are avail- able, we show how to optimally adjust any learned predictorterm·key so as to remove discriminationterm·dom according to our definition. Our framework also improves incentives by shifting the cost of poor classificationterm·key from disadvantaged groups to the decision maker, who can respond by improving the classificationterm·key accuracy.

In line with other studies, our notion is obliviousterm·key: it depends only on the joint statistics of the predictorterm·key, the target and the protected attributeterm·dom, but not on interpretation of individual features. We study the inherent limits of defining and identifying biases based on such obliviousterm·key measures, outlining what can and cannot be inferred from different obliviousterm·key tests.

We illustrate our notion using a case study of FICO credit scores.

1 Introduction

As machine learning increasingly affects decisions in domains protected by anti-discriminationterm·dom law, there is much interest in algorithmically measuring and ensuring fairnessterm·mth in machine learning. In domains such as advertising, credit, employment, education, and criminal justice, machine learning could help obtain more accurate predictions, but its effect on existing biases is not well understood. Although reliance on data and quantitative measures can help quantify and eliminate existing biases, some scholars caution that algorithms can also introduce new biases or perpetuate existing ones [BS16]. In May 2014, the Obama Administration’s Big Data Working Group released a report [PPM+14] arguing that discriminationterm·dom can sometimes “be the inadvertent outcome of the way big data technologies are structured and used” and pointed toward “the potential of encoding discriminationterm·dom in automated decisions”. A subsequent White House report [Whi16] calls for “equal opportunity by designterm·def” as a guiding principle in domains such as credit scoring.

Despite the demand, a vetted methodology for avoiding discriminationterm·dom against protected attributes in machine learning is lacking. A naïve approach might require that the algorithm should ignore all protected attributes such as race, color, religion, gender, disability, or family status. However, this idea of “fairness through unawarenessterm·def” is ineffective due to the existence of redundant encodings, ways of predicting protected attributes from other features [PRT08].

Another common conception of non-discriminationterm·dom is demographic parityterm·key. Demographic parityterm·key requires that a decision—such as accepting or denying a loan application—be independent of the protected attributeterm·dom. In the case of a binary decision (cid:98)Y ∈ {0, 1} and a binary protected attribute Aterm·var ∈ {0, 1}, this constraint can be formalized by asking that Pr{(cid:98)Y = 1 | A = 0} = Pr{(cid:98)Y =

1 | A = 1}. In other words, membership in a protected class should have no correlation with the decision. Through its various equivalent formalizations this idea appears in numerous papers. Unfortunately, as was already argued by Dwork et al. [DHP+12], the notion is seriously flawed on two counts. First, it doesn’t ensure fairnessterm·mth. Indeed, the notion permits that we accept qualified applicants in the demographic A = 0, but unqualified individuals in A = 1, so long as the percentages of acceptance match. This behavior can arise naturally, when there is little or no training data available within A = 1. Second, demographic parityterm·key often cripples the utility that we might hope to achieve. Just imagine the common scenario in which the target variable Y —whether an individual actually defaults or not—is correlated with A. Demographic parityterm·key would not allow the ideal predictorterm·key (cid:98)Y = Y , which can hardly be considered discriminatory as it represents the actual outcome. As a result, the loss in utility of introducing demographic parityterm·key can be substantial.

In this paper, we consider non-discriminationterm·dom from the perspective of supervised learningterm·ai, where the goal is to predict a true outcome Y from features Xterm·var based on labeled training data, while ensuring they are “non-discriminatory” with respect to a specified protected attribute Aterm·var. As in the usual supervised learningterm·ai setting, we assume that we have access to labeled training data, in our case indicating also the protected attribute Aterm·var. That is, to samples from the joint distribution of (X, A, Y ). This data is used to construct a predictorterm·key (cid:98)Y (X) or (cid:98)Y (X, A), and we also use such data to test whether it is unfairly discriminatory.

Unlike demographic parityterm·key, our notion always allows for the perfectly accurate solution of (cid:98)Y = Y . More broadly, our criterion is easier to achieve the more accurate the predictorterm·key (cid:98)Y is, aligning fairnessterm·mth with the central goal in supervised learningterm·ai of building more accurate predictors.

The notion we propose is “obliviousterm·key”, in that it is based only on the joint distribution, or joint statistics, of the true target Yterm·var , the predictions (cid:98)Y , and the protected attribute Aterm·var. In particular, it does not evaluate the features in X nor the functional form of the predictorterm·key (cid:98)Y (X) nor how it was derived. This matches other tests recently proposed and conducted, including demographic parityterm·key and different analyses of common risk scores. In many cases, only obliviousterm·key analysis is possible as the functional form of the score and underlying training data are not public. The only information about the score is the score itself, which can then be correlated with the target and protected attributeterm·dom. Furthermore, even if the features or the functional form are available, going beyond obliviousterm·key analysis essentially requires subjective interpretation or casual assumptions about specific features, which we aim to avoid.

1.1 Summary of our contributions

We propose a simple, interpretable, and actionable framework for measuring and removing discriminationterm·dom based on protected attributes. We argue that, unlike demographic parityterm·key, our framework provides a meaningful measure of discriminationterm·dom, while demonstrating in theory and experiment that we also achieve much higher utility. Our key contributions are as follows:

• We propose an easily checkable and interpretable notion of avoiding discriminationterm·dom based on protected attributes. Our notion enjoys a natural interpretation in terms of graphical dependency models. It can also be viewed as shifting the burden of uncertainty in classificationterm·key from the protected class to the decision maker. In doing so, our notion helps to incentivize the collection of better features, that depend more directly on the target rather then the protected attributeterm·dom, and of data that allows better prediction for all protected classes.

• We give a simple and effective framework for constructing classifiers satisfying our cri- terion from an arbitrary learned predictorterm·key. Rather than changing a possibly complex training pipeline, the result follows via a simple post-processing step that minimizes the loss in utility.

• We show that the Bayes optimal non-discriminating (according to our definition) classifier is the classifier derived from any Bayes optimal (not necessarily non-discriminating) regressor using our post-processing step. Moreover, we quantify the loss that follows from imposing our non-discriminationterm·dom condition in case the score we start from deviates from Bayesianterm·stat optimality. This result helps to justify the approach of deriving a fair classifier via post-processing rather than changing the original training process.

• We capture the inherent limitations of our approach, as well as any other obliviousterm·key ap- proach, through a non-identifiability result showing that different dependency structures with possibly different intuitive notions of fairnessterm·mth cannot be separated based on any oblivious notionterm·def or test.

Throughout our work, we assume a source distribution over (Y , X, A), where Y is the target or true outcome (e.g. “default on loan”), X are the available features, and A is the protected attributeterm·dom. Generally, the features Xterm·var may be an arbitrary vector or an abstract object, such as an image. Our work does not refer to the particular form X has.

The objective of supervised learningterm·ai is to construct a (possibly randomized) predictorterm·key (cid:98)Y = f (X, A) that predicts Y as is typically measured through a loss function. Furthermore, we would like to require that (cid:98)Y does not discriminate with respect to A, and the goal of this paper is to formalize this notion.

2.2 Real-valued scores

Even if the target is binary, a real-valued predictive score R = f (X, A) is often used (e.g. FICO scores for predicting loan default), with the interpretation that higher values of R correspond to greater likelihood of Y = 1 and thus a bias toward predicting (cid:98)Y = 1. 2.3 Obliviousterm·key measures

As stated before, our notions of non-discriminationterm·dom are obliviousterm·key in the following formal sense.

Definition 2.3. A property of a predictorterm·key (cid:98)Y or score R is said to be obliviousterm·key if it only depends on the joint distribution of (Y , A, (cid:98)Y ) or (Y , A, R), respectively.

As a consequence of being obliviousterm·key, all the information we need to verify our definitions is contained in the joint distribution of predictorterm·key, protected group and outcome, ((cid:98)Y , A, Y ). In the binary case, when A and Y are reasonably well balanced, the joint distribution of ((cid:98)Y , A, Y ) is determined by 8 parameters that can be estimated to very high accuracy from samples. We will therefore ignore the effect of finite sample perturbations and instead assume that we know the joint distribution of ((cid:98)Y , A, Y ).

4.2 Deriving from a score function

We now consider deriving non-discriminating predictors from a real valued score R ∈ [0, 1]. The motivation is that in many realistic scenarios (such as FICO scores), the data are summarized by a one-dimensional score function and a decision is made based on the score, typically by thresholding it. Since a continuous statistic can carry more information than a binary outcome Y , we can hope to achieve higher utility when working with R directly, rather then with a binary predictorterm·key (cid:98)Y .

A “protected attributeterm·dom blind” way of deriving a binary predictorterm·key from R would be to threshold it, i.e. using (cid:98)Y = I {R > t}. We examine various fairnessterm·mth measures in the context of FICO scores with the protected attributeterm·dom of race. FICO scores are a proprietary classifier widely used in the United States to predict credit worthiness. Our FICO data is based on a sample of 301536 TransUnion TransRisk scores from 2003 [Res07]. Incentives. Requiring equalized oddsterm·mth creates an incentive structure for the entity building the predictorterm·key that aligns well with achieving fairnessterm·mth. Achieving better prediction with equalized oddsterm·mth requires collecting features that more directly capture the target Yterm·var , unrelated to its correlation with the protected attributeterm·dom. Deriving an equalized oddsterm·mth predictorterm·key from a score involves considering the pointwise minimum ROC curve among different protected groups, encouraging constructing of predictors that are accurate in all groups, e.g., by collecting data appropriately or basing prediction on features predictive in all groups.

When to use our post-processing step. An important feature of our notion is that it can be achieved via a simple and efficient post-processing step. In fact, this step requires only aggregate information about the data and therefore could even be carried out in a privacy- preserving manner (formally, via Differential Privacy). In contrast, many other approaches require changing a usually complex machine learning training pipeline, or require access to raw data. Despite its simplicity, our post-processing step exhibits a strong optimality principle. If the underlying score was close to optimal, then the derived predictorterm·key will be close to optimal among all predictors satisfying our definition. However, this does not mean that the predictorterm·key is necessarily good in an absolute sense. It also does not mean that the loss compared to the original predictorterm·key is always small. An alternative to using our post-processing step is always to invest in better features and more data. Only when this is no longer an option, should our post-processing step be applied.

Predictive affirmative action. In some situations, including Scenario II in Section 6, the equalized oddsterm·mth predictorterm·key can be thought of as introducing some sort of affirmative action: the ∗ is shifted based on A. This shift compensates for the fact that, optimally predictive score R ∗ is more due to uncertainty, the score is in a sense more biased then the target label (roughly, R correlated with A then Y is correlated with A). Informally speaking, our approach transfers the burden of uncertainty from the protected class to the decision maker. We believe this is a reasonable proposal, since it incentivizes the decision maker to invest additional resources toward building a better model.
